THE SECOND LIMB OF YOGA
Niyama
the inner observances · Yoga Sutra 2.32

Turning inward

The second limb, after the yamas

The niyamas are the second limb of yoga, the inner observances. Where the yamas point outward to how we meet the world, the niyamas turn toward how we tend ourselves. In sutra 2.32 Patanjali names five: śauca (purity), santoṣa (contentment), tapas (disciplined effort), svādhyāya (self-study), and īśvara-praṇidhāna (surrender to the divine). Yoga International reads them as constructive tools for cultivating steadiness and self-confidence, with occasions to practice them arising in an ordinary day.

The niyamas are woven together with the yamas rather than stacked on top of them. Purity clears the mind, contentment settles it, disciplined effort tempers it, self-study turns it toward its own depths, and surrender releases it into something larger. The five build toward the last, which this site takes up at length under The Divine.

ON SOURCES

This page draws on Yoga International’s overview of the five niyamas and on Maetreyii Ma’s Living Love: The Yoga of Yama and Niyama (Ananda Gurukula, 2016), alongside the sutras themselves. Translations of the niyamas vary, and different lineages weigh them differently. Where a reading is one interpretation among several, it is offered as that.

The five niyamas

Each with its sutra, its meaning, and its practice
Sauchaśauca
Purity of body and mind
Tap to open the teaching
Sattva-śuddhi-saumanasya-aikāgrya-indriya-jaya-ātma-darśana-yogyatvāni ca. From purity of heart follow cheerfulness, one-pointedness, mastery of the senses, and the fitness to know the Self.YOGA SUTRA 2.41

Saucha is the practice of cleansing, of both the body and the mind. Maetreyii Ma describes it as clearing away the clutter that settles on the mind so that a clearer seeing becomes possible, the way a cleaned window lets in more light. Yoga International frames the practice as choosing wisely among the food, the emotions, and the thoughts we let in, since each of these becomes part of us. Purity here is care rather than perfection: an ongoing tending, held lightly, that leaves us clearer than it found us.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, saucha is a clean, attentive practice: a clear space, an unhurried breath, the body cared for rather than pushed. In meditation, it is the settling of muddied water until the depths grow visible. In daily life, it asks what we take in, the food, the images, the running commentary of the mind, and invites us to choose what leaves us clearer.

Santoshasantoṣa
Contentment
Tap to open the teaching
Santoṣād anuttamaḥ sukha-lābhaḥ. From contentment comes unsurpassed happiness.YOGA SUTRA 2.42

Santosha is contentment, an acceptance of our life and of ourselves as they are in this moment. Yoga International describes it as the recognition that whatever we have in the present is enough, which makes happiness a practice rather than a circumstance we wait on. Maetreyii Ma pairs it with saucha as the clear waters of the mind, and names the barrier plainly: when we are wrapped up in our own fears and wants, even a calm mind cannot see to its own depths. Contentment is what quiets that self-absorption.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, santosha meets the body it has today, without measuring it against yesterday or against anyone else on a neighboring mat. In meditation, it lets this breath, this state, be enough, so the practice stops reaching for a better experience than the one that is here. In daily life, it loosens the grip of comparison and craving, and lets sufficiency be something we notice rather than something we earn.

Tapastapas
Disciplined effort
Tap to open the teaching
Kāya-indriya-siddhir aśuddhi-kṣayāt tapasaḥ. Through disciplined effort, impurity dwindles and the body and senses are refined.YOGA SUTRA 2.43

Tapas means heat, the warmth generated by steady, willing effort. Yoga International reads it as discipline freely accepted, the effort that turns intention into change, present in asana and meditation and in the smallest chosen task. Maetreyii Ma cautions against a harsh reading of it. In her account, true tapas focuses energy through love rather than through self-punishment, burning away the small, defended sense of self in the fire of a larger devotion. The heat is real, and it can be kind.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, tapas is the steady effort that shows up on the mat on the ordinary days, sustainable rather than punishing. In meditation, it is the discipline of returning, again and again, without drama. In daily life, Yoga International suggests holding to one or two changes we can actually keep, and meeting even mundane tasks with full attention, so that discipline becomes a warmth we live in rather than a burden we resent.

Svadhyayasvādhyāya
Self-study
Tap to open the teaching
Svādhyāyād iṣṭa-devatā-samprayogaḥ. Through self-study, one draws near the chosen ideal.YOGA SUTRA 2.44

Svadhyaya joins sva, the self, with a root meaning to reflect, study, and contemplate, so the word holds both the study of sacred texts and the study of oneself. Yoga International describes it as the effort to know the innermost core of our being through contemplation, applied and tested in daily life. Maetreyii Ma reads it as reflection upon the nature of the soul, the Atman, the search that turns knowledge inward until it becomes self-knowledge. The two studies feed each other: a text read closely returns us to ourselves, and honest self-observation sends us back to the text.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, svadhyaya is honest witnessing of the body, what it is asking for and what it is bracing against, read without decoration. In meditation, it is self-inquiry, watching the mind well enough to begin to know it. In daily life, it pairs the study of texts that steady us with plain observation of our own patterns, asking whether the way we are living matches what we say we value.

Ishvara Pranidhanaīśvara-praṇidhāna
Surrender to the divine
Tap to open the teaching
Samādhi-siddhir īśvara-praṇidhānāt. From surrender to the divine comes the attainment of samadhi.YOGA SUTRA 2.45

Ishvara pranidhana is trustful surrender to the divine, and Yoga International names it the highest of the niyamas. It is a giving-over rather than a giving-up: a release of the small self’s insistence on controlling every outcome, in favor of a larger purpose. Maetreyii Ma describes it as self-surrender in deep contemplation of the eternal source of all, and notes that people have named that source differently across history while pointing toward the same thing, a consciousness greater than our individual sense of self. This site takes up that question, and the many doors into it, at length under The Divine.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, ishvara pranidhana offers the practice rather than performs it, letting the effort be given rather than displayed. In meditation, it is the letting go into something vast enough to hold us, the release that the deepest absorption asks for. In daily life, it echoes the Gita’s core instruction: to do the work fully and release our claim on its fruits, trusting what we cannot control to something larger than ourselves.

What the research sees

Peer-reviewed work on the ethical and observant limbs

Like the yamas, the niyamas are among the least studied limbs of yoga in modern research. A starting shelf:

Impact of yama and niyama on psychospiritual factors in young adults: a randomized controlled trial
XU, ITAGI & THAIYAR · INT. J. YOGA (PHILOSOPHY, PSYCHOLOGY & PARAPSYCHOLOGY), 2021
One hundred young adults, randomized to three months of yama-niyama training or control. The training group gained on sattva and dropped on rajas and tamas (Vedic Personality Inventory, P<0.001). Early evidence that the observant limbs are trainable, with measurable change in disposition.
From Yama to Samyama
INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF YOGA, 2016 · 9(2), 95–96
Argues that the eight limbs are meant to be practiced together rather than climbed in sequence, the basis of this site’s invitation to begin anywhere. The niyamas deepen the yamas rather than follow them.
Living Love: The Yoga of Yama and Niyama
MAETREYII MA · ANANDA GURUKULA PUBLICATIONS, 2016
A practitioner’s book-length treatment of all ten yamas and niyamas as teachings for daily life. Read here for the framing of tapas as focused love and of svadhyaya as reflection on the soul. A teaching text rather than a study, and offered as one.
