Dharana

dhāraṇā · the sixth limb

Gathering the whole mind onto a single point, the art of focus that becomes meditation

The bridge limb

Binding the mind to one point

Patanjali defines dharana in a single line: desha-bandhah chittasya dharana, the binding of the mind-field to one place (Yoga Sutra 3.1). After the senses have been gathered inward through pratyahara, dharana gives the quieted mind one thing to hold. The Sanskrit root dhri means to hold, to bear, to keep steady. Dharana is the holding.

The quality it builds is ekagrata, one-pointedness. A scattered mind is like sunlight spread across a room, warm but diffuse. Concentration is the same light gathered through a lens onto a single point, where it begins to burn. Nothing about the mind changes except its gathering, and everything changes.

Dharana is the bridge. Hold the point until the holding becomes effortless and unbroken, and concentration ripens into dhyana, meditation. Let meditation deepen until the one who watches and the thing watched dissolve into each other, and that is samadhi. Patanjali names these three together as samyama, the inner instrument of yoga.

Contemplative science studies this limb directly. Lutz, Slagter, Dunne and Davidson (2008) sort meditative practices by how they handle attention, and dharana sits squarely in the family they call focused attention: one object held, attention returned each time it wanders, the monitoring of that wandering growing sharper with practice. The muscle of return is measurable, and it strengthens.

The objects of focus

Where the mind can rest

Dharana needs an object, a single place to bind the mind. The object can be outside you or within. Any of these is a complete doorway; choose one and return to it again and again.

Trataka

trāṭaka · steady gazing

Resting the open gaze on a candle flame without blinking until the eyes soften and fill, then holding the after-image within. The Hatha Yoga Pradipika (2.31) names it a cleanser of the eyes and the mind.

Yoga International: Trataka ↗

Drishti

dṛṣṭi · the yogic gaze

The focal gaze points used inside the asanas: the tip of the nose, the horizon, the thumb. Drishti turns a physical pose into a moving dharana, and threads concentration through the whole Asana practice.

Yoga Journal: Drishti ↗

The breath

anapana · the anchor

Counting and following each wave of the breath, returning the moment attention drifts. The breath is the most portable point of all, always present, always moving. It carries dharana straight into the Breath page.

Wisdomlib: Ekagrata ↗

Bija mantra & japa

japa · a single sound

Repeating a seed-sound or sacred phrase, silently or aloud, so a single sound becomes the point the mind holds. Each repetition gathers the attention a little more completely.

Wisdomlib: Japa ↗

Yantra & mandala

yantra · a geometric image

Resting the eyes and mind on a sacred geometric form, letting its center draw the attention inward toward the still point at the heart of the pattern.

Wisdomlib: Yantra ↗

A center in the body

bindu · an inner point

Fixing attention on a single energetic center: the space between the brows, the heart, the navel. The point is felt rather than seen, and the body itself becomes the field of focus.

A first practice

Trataka: the gathered flame

Trataka is the most direct way to taste ekagrata, because the eyes give the mind something simple to hold. A candle is enough.

Practice · candle gazing
One flame, one mind
  1. Sit tall in a dim, still room. Place a lit candle about an arm’s length away, the flame level with your eyes.
  2. Rest your open gaze softly on the brightest part of the flame. Let the eyes be steady and relaxed, blinking as little as feels comfortable.
  3. When a thought arrives, notice it without following, and return the whole of your attention to the flame. This returning is the practice.
  4. After a minute or two, gently close the eyes and hold the glowing after-image of the flame at the center of your inner vision for as long as it lasts.
  5. When the image fades, open the eyes and begin again, two or three rounds. Close by letting the gaze and the breath rest soft.

Begin with a few minutes. The point is steadiness, and steadiness grows quietly. If the eyes strain, soften the gaze or shorten the gazing and lengthen the inner holding.

The effect is measurable even in a single sitting. In a 2021 repeated-measures study, one session of trataka improved performance on the Corsi block-tapping task, a standard test of visuospatial attention and working memory, over a matched control condition. The gathered light does gather the mind.

The connective tissue

Dharana woven through the whole practice

Once you recognize dharana, you find it everywhere. The drishti in a standing pose is dharana. The breath followed in pranayama is dharana. The mantra carried in chant is dharana. The same single gesture, gathering attention onto one point, wears many forms across the limbs, and each form trains the same quiet muscle of return.

This is why concentration is called the bridge. It connects the body of Asana to the stillness of meditation, and it connects the outer practices to the inner ones. To strengthen dharana in any single place is to strengthen it in all of them.

Samyama · the inner trinity

When dharana, dhyana, and samadhi flow together on one object, the tradition calls it samyama (Yoga Sutra 3.4), the complete gathering. The Yoga Sutras describe samyama as the master key of the inner practice, the means by which the deepest knowing becomes available. It begins, always, with the simple act of holding one point.
