Meditation · The Living Practice

Inner growth that expands like the universe

The banyan tree carries much of this tradition's wisdom, picturing inner growth as a trunk rising year by year toward the light. Meditation adds a second image alongside it. Here growth expands in every direction at once, the way the universe expands, with no edge and no center that can be pointed to, each moment of stillness opening more space than the one before.

The cosmos holds every direction within it. When you sit and the breath grows quiet and the mind settles into ease, you begin to notice this directly. The stillness opens you wider. What seemed a small interior reveals itself as a doorway, and beyond it lies more room than the visible world seems to contain.

The tradition gives this expansion a structure, so that we do not get lost in it. It names four reaches that meditation can travel: down into the subconscious where the roots of who we are lie waiting, into the material world where the body sits breathing on the ground, up through the metaphysical where the subtle and the symbolic live, and out into the world beyond, the absolute realm where nothing is relative. The map of those reaches lives on the Introspect page, where the senses first turn inward; their furthest edge has its own page in Samadhi. This page carries the practices themselves, the breath that moves between the reaches, and the two great rivers, Vedic and Buddhist, that have charted this territory for thousands of years.

Vedic & Buddhist approaches

Two rivers from one mountain

Meditation as we practice it flows from two great rivers. The older is the Vedic stream, the meditation of the Upanishads, the Yoga Sutras, and Vedanta. The younger, born from the same mountain roughly twenty-five centuries ago, is the Buddhist stream. The Buddha was raised in the world of Vedic practice and trained under its teachers before he set out on his own. To understand where the two part is to understand the deepest question meditation asks: what, exactly, is found at the center?

The Vedic stream

Atman · the Self that is found

The Vedic path holds that beneath the changing layers of body, breath, and thought lies atman, the true Self: unchanging, eternal, pure awareness. Meditation peels back the koshas, the sheaths that cover it, until the Self stands revealed and is recognized as one with Brahman, the ground of all being.

The journey is a homecoming. You travel inward to find what was always there. The drop discovers it was the ocean the whole time.

The Buddhist stream

Anatta · the Self that is not found

The Buddha looked for that permanent Self with the same rigor and reported that he could not find it. This is anatta, not-self: where we expect a fixed essence, there is instead a flowing process, five ever-changing bundles of form, feeling, perception, formation, and consciousness, the skandhas, with no unchanging owner behind them.

The journey is a release. You look for the self so closely that the search itself dissolves the illusion of a separate one. The drop sees that it was never separate to begin with.

Where the rivers part

This is the single great divergence, and it is worth naming clearly. The Vedic tradition meditates toward the Self; the Buddhist tradition meditates through the idea of a self until it lets go. Atman (an eternal Self) and anatta (no eternal self) are the names of the two answers. Almost everything else they share: the ethics, the breath, the stages of concentration, the goal of freedom from suffering. They diverge on the nature of what is realized at the bottom of the stillness. The Buddha kept the entire Vedic architecture of practice and removed its keystone, the permanent Self, and built a path that needs no keystone to stand.

Held side by side, the two read as a koan the practice itself resolves. Many who go deep report that at a certain depth the difference between "the Self I found" and "the self that dissolved" stops meaning very much. Both traditions agree that whatever is there is not the small, anxious "I" that sat down to meditate. You may practice from either bank of the river. The water is the same.

Vedic & Buddhist psychology

The mind, and the one who watches it

Both traditions are, before anything else, sciences of the mind. And both begin from the same startling claim: the awareness that knows you are thinking stands distinct from the mind that thinks. Meditation is the practice of telling them apart.

The Vedic instrument

Vedic psychology does not speak of a single "mind." It describes an inner instrument, the antahkarana, with distinct functions. Manas is the sensory, restless, processing mind, the one that receives impressions and chatters. Buddhi is the discerning intellect, the faculty that decides and discriminates. Ahamkara is the I-maker, the function that stamps "mine" onto experience. And chitta is the deeper storehouse where impressions settle. The Yoga Sutras define yoga itself in one line as chitta vritti nirodha, the stilling of the whirlpools of the mind-field.

The crucial move is this: all of these are instruments. They are not the knower. Behind them, witnessing them, is atman, pure consciousness, the seer that the mind serves but cannot become. The cognition is the activity; the consciousness is what the activity happens within. To confuse the two, the tradition says, is the root of all suffering: we mistake the weather for the sky.

The Buddhist analysis

Buddhist psychology, the Abhidharma, performs a similar dissection and arrives at a subtly different place. It too distinguishes citta, the basic knowing or awareness, from manas, the mental processing that clings and constructs a sense of "I," from the mental factors that color each moment. But because there is no atman, no permanent witness, it does not place an eternal seer behind the process. Awareness here is itself a stream, a continuity of momentary knowings, each one arising and passing with its object.

So the divergence in psychology mirrors the divergence in metaphysics. The Vedic practitioner learns to rest as the witness, the unchanging awareness behind the mind. The Buddhist practitioner learns to watch awareness itself flow, finding freedom in seeing that there is nothing to stand apart, only this, arising and releasing, breath after breath.

The shared insight

Strip away the disagreement and a single practical teaching remains, the one your own meditation will show you within minutes: thought is something that happens, like a sound or a sensation, and you are the space in which it is noticed. That space is calm and wide, the steady ground beneath its passing weather. Whether you call it the eternal Self or the flowing stream of knowing, learning to rest there is the whole art.
