Samadhi

samādhi · the eighth limb

The meditator and the world merge: the alchemy where practice becomes being

Begin here

Sitting at the edge of absorption

Samadhi arrives; it resists being performed. What a practitioner can do is set the conditions and stop interfering. This sitting practice walks to the edge and waits there.

Practice · from holding to absorption
Releasing the effort of watching
  1. Sit steadily and let the breath settle. Gather the senses inward as on the Introspect page, and give the mind one object to hold, a breath, a flame’s after-image, a mantra, as in dharana.
  2. Hold the object until the holding smooths into an unbroken flow. This is dhyana, meditation: attention pouring toward one thing like oil poured from one vessel to another.
  3. Now notice the last piece of effort left: the sense of being someone who watches. Release even that. Let the object, the watching, and the watcher soften toward each other.
  4. If a moment comes when only the object remains, shining without a commentary, let it be. It may last a breath. The tradition says that breath is samadhi’s first taste.
  5. When the ordinary mind returns, and it will, welcome it back without grasping at what happened. Absorption deepens through repetition, released each time.

Patanjali describes the arc in three sutras. Concentration binds the mind to one place; meditation is the unbroken flow of attention there; samadhi is when the object alone shines and the mind “is as if emptied of itself” (Yoga Sutras 3.1–3.3). Held together the three are samyama, and the whole path has been walking toward this gathering.

The texts

What the tradition means by absorption

The Yoga Sutras open with samadhi; the first of its four books is the Samadhi Pada. Patanjali distinguishes absorption that still carries a seed of thought, samprajnata, absorption with support, from the seedless stillness beyond it, asamprajnata, where even the subtlest movement of mind has settled. The distinction matters practically: early tastes of samadhi keep an object and a flavor of knowing, and the deeper reaches release both.

Scholars have argued for a century about how these stages map onto experience. Ian Kesarcodi-Watson’s close reading in Philosophy East and West (1982) untangles the terms with care, and a 2024 comparative study by Randika Perera reads samadhi across the Buddhist and Yogic traditions, where the same ladder of absorption appears as the jhanas. The two traditions climb by nearly identical rungs and name different summits: the Yogic path centers consciousness in the true self, purusha, while the Buddhist path finds no self to center. The divergence is the same one traced in the two rivers of meditation.

In practice surveys, samadhi is the limb teachers claim least. In a 2024 study of yoga teachers, samadhi ranked lowest in reported knowledge of all eight limbs (62.2%), and the authors set it apart from the practice questions entirely, since it is the fruit of the whole eightfold path rather than a technique to schedule. An early controlled report on a meditation program named for this limb, Siddha Samadhi Yoga, found reduced anxiety and depression symptoms in a small preliminary trial (Kozasa et al. 2008, Psychological Reports; the journal keeps the article paywalled). The honest summary: the state itself remains beyond the instruments, and what can be measured is the path toward it.

The world beyond · the absolute realm

Sat-Chit-Ananda: where nothing is relative

At the furthest reach of the practice, past the roots and the ground and the branches, the tradition points to the absolute realm, the one place where nothing is relative to anything else. It cannot be described, only approached, and the approach is made through three words that are not really three. Sat-chit-ananda: being, consciousness, bliss. The name Vedanta gives to the nature of the absolute itself.

Sat
Being · Eternal

Pure existence, beyond time. Timelessness itself: that which simply is, untouched by beginning or end.

Chit
Consciousness · Awake

Pure awareness, fully conscious of all things and all beings. The light by which anything is known at all.

Ananda
Bliss · Ecstasy

Boundless joy, bliss, euphoria. The very texture of reality when nothing obstructs it.

This is what the tradition means by enlightenment, moksha, liberation: a realm entered, in which eternal being, total awareness, and unbroken bliss are revealed to be one single thing, our own deepest nature, never absent, only overlooked. In the map of the four reaches on the Introspect page, this is the universe at the top of the diagram and the depth at the bottom meeting and turning out to be the same place.

A bridge across traditions

Many who come to this practice from a Christian background find a familiar shape here. The absolute realm of sat-chit-ananda, eternal, all-knowing, and full of bliss, rhymes with what some Christian mystics have meant by heaven or by union with God: not a place reached after death so much as a reality always present, glimpsed when the veil thins. The vocabulary differs and the theologies are not identical. But the testimony of those who have touched it, in either language, is remarkably consistent: timeless, radiant, and at peace.

It is worth saying plainly that this realm is already and eternally the case, waiting only to be uncovered. The waves of the breath, the ethics of the yamas, the gathering of the mind: each one clears the obstructions to it. The work of meditation is to stop standing in its own light, so what was always present can shine through. And if a lifetime of practice does not arrive there, the tradition offers a quiet reassurance, told as a story between Arjuna and Krishna, that no step taken toward it is ever lost.
