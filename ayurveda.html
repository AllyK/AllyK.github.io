Ayurveda · the knowledge of life

A sibling science

Ayurveda joins ayus, life, with veda, knowledge. It is a complete medical system with its own canon, its own institutions, and its own history, and it is older as a written tradition than Patanjali’s eight limbs. It is often described as yoga’s sister science, which is accurate as long as sister means a separate person.

That is why this material has a page rather than a box on every limb. Yoga and Ayurveda grew alongside each other, share a vocabulary of prana, gunas, and elements, and disagree about plenty. Folding one into the other flattens both, and it happens to be the exact move that scholars use to identify a modern, Western reading of Ayurveda. More on that below, because this site would rather name the pattern it is standing in.

The classical canon is three texts, and all three are now on the shelf behind this page. The Charaka Samhita is the physician’s book, concerned with internal medicine, diagnosis, and ethics. The Sushruta Samhita is the surgeon’s book, and it is startling: it describes cataract couching, rhinoplasty, and the 107 marma, the vital points where flesh, vessels, tendons, bones and joints meet. The Ashtanga Hridayam is the later synthesis, and the one most practitioners actually study. Dominik Wujastyk’s The Roots of Ayurveda translates the key passages and is the honest entry point.

The three doshas

Not types of people, patterns of behaviour

The doshas are usually introduced as personality types, which is where most of the trouble starts. Classically they are closer to functional principles: three ways matter and energy behave, present in everyone in different proportion. Vata moves, pitta transforms, kapha holds. You have all three, you need all three, and none of them is a horoscope.

The distinction that does the real work is between prakriti, the constitution you were born with and keep for life, and vikriti, what is out of balance right now. Prakriti is a fixed baseline; vikriti is today’s weather. Practically, most of what a practice can address is vikriti, and most of what people call “my dosha” is actually the imbalance they are currently living inside.

The gunas, the twenty qualities, are what the system actually reasons with: dry and oily, cold and hot, heavy and light, rough and smooth, and so on. Treatment follows one rule, like increases like, and opposites balance. A cold, dry, mobile body in a cold, dry, windy month, eating cold, dry, raw food, is not being mysterious when it feels cold, dry, and unsettled. That is the whole logic, and it is more sensible than its reputation.

Dinacharya · daily routine

The part with the least mysticism and the most evidence

If one piece of Ayurveda deserves attention from someone sceptical, it is dinacharya: the claim that the body runs on rhythms, and that keeping regular ones is itself medicine. Wake near the same hour. Eat the largest meal in the middle of the day, when digestion is strongest. Wind down as the light goes. Sleep before the second wind arrives.

Nothing there requires a metaphysics. Circadian science arrived at strikingly similar conclusions from an entirely different direction, and meal timing and consistent sleep windows are among the better-supported lifestyle findings of the last two decades. Ayurveda got there by observation, several thousand years earlier, and filed it under ritucharya and dinacharya instead of chronobiology. Cate Stillman’s Body Thrive is the most practical modern treatment, and Bri Maya Tiwari’s The Path of Practice the most beautiful.

The other durable pieces are similar in character: abhyanga, self-massage with warm oil, which is touch and downtime whatever else it is; tongue scraping; warm water; eating without a screen. These ask nothing of your bloodstream, which is the line this site draws.

Ahara · food

The part everyone means when they say Ayurveda

Ask most people what Ayurveda is and you will get an answer about food. That is not a distortion. Wujastyk, introducing the classical texts, notes plainly that “great attention is given to diet in ayurveda.” Food is the system’s primary medicine, and it is where its logic is easiest to follow and easiest to sell.

It starts with agni. This is the piece popular writing skips, and it inverts the usual question. Ayurveda is less interested in what you eat than in what you can digest. Agni is the digestive fire, and it is the hinge of the whole system: strong agni turns ordinary food into ojas, vitality; weak agni turns even excellent food into ama, the undigested residue the texts blame for a great deal of illness. A perfect meal eaten by a wrecked digestion is not a perfect meal. Practically this means the tradition cares enormously about how you eat: warm rather than cold, cooked rather than raw when agni is low, sitting down, unhurried, without a screen, and not again before the last meal has cleared.

Then the six tastes. This is the actual reasoning engine, and it is more interesting than the dosha lists it gets flattened into. Every food carries one or more of six tastes, each built from elements, each with a predictable effect. The classical instruction is that a full meal contains all six, which is a quietly good piece of nutritional advice: it makes a plate of only sweet and salty, which is to say most processed food, structurally incomplete.

The rule underneath is the one from the doshas: like increases like, and opposites balance. A cold, dry, mobile body in a cold, dry month eating cold, dry, raw salad is not a mystery when it feels cold, dry and unsettled, and the answer is warm, oily, cooked, and sweet in the classical sense rather than the sugar sense. A hot, sharp person is not helped by chilli and black coffee at noon in July. Kapha, heavy and cool, is the one constitution that actually wants the bitter greens and the ginger. This is not a magic system. It is pattern-matching, done for two thousand years, and it holds up better than most of what replaced it.

The timing rule matters as much as the content, and it is the claim that has aged best. The largest meal belongs at midday, when the tradition says agni is highest; the evening meal should be lighter and earlier than most of us manage. Chrononutrition research arrived at a strikingly similar place from an entirely different direction: eating in the body’s active phase lines up with peak insulin sensitivity and glucose tolerance, while late-night eating is associated with impaired glucose metabolism and increased fat storage. Of everything on this page, this is the piece with the strongest independent support, and Ayurveda got there by watching people, several thousand years early.

The vegetarian assumption is modern, and the texts say otherwise

This is the correction most worth having, and it comes from the most authoritative source on the shelf. Nearly every Western presentation of Ayurveda assumes it is vegetarian, usually via sattvic food and ahimsa. The classical texts do not support that. Dominik Wujastyk, introducing his translations in The Roots of Ayurveda:

“To someone acquainted with contemporary Hinduism, it may come as a surprise to see that the meat of many kinds of animals is recommended as a normal food. In fact the real surprise is not so much that meat is considered part of the diet, but that its use is presented so completely without apology or explanation. The texts of Caraka and Suśruta take it utterly for granted, and apparently feel no need even to attempt a justification.” DOMINIK WUJASTYK, THE ROOTS OF AYURVEDA (PENGUIN CLASSICS), INTRODUCTION

He goes further, and the detail is delicious. The tension with ahimsa was noticed not by the original authors but by a commentator a thousand years later: the eleventh-century Cakrapaṇidatta observed that a physician told to cultivate benevolent fellow-feeling is “not demonstrating much benevolence to living creatures if he prescribes the fresh meat of young animals to his patients,” and is “quite obviously implicated in harming a living creature.” Yet meat is what Ayurveda prescribes for consumption and as ordinary diet.

None of that is an argument for eating meat. It is an argument against a marketing claim. “Ayurveda says eat this way” usually means “a reformist, largely modern, often Brahminical reading of Ayurveda says eat this way,” and the two should not wear the same clothes. If you are vegetarian for ahimsa, that is a yogic and ethical commitment and a good one. It is simply not what Charaka wrote.

Purity, and who gets to be pure

The sattvic, rajasic, and tamasic food scheme comes from the Gita and Samkhya rather than from the medical texts, and in South Asia the language of dietary purity has never been separable from caste. Rules about what is pure, who may cook it, and whose touch pollutes it are among the mechanisms by which caste is enforced daily. Thenmozhi Soundararajan’s The Trauma of Caste is unsparing about how those rules land on Dalit bodies. A Western practitioner adopting purity language as a wellness aesthetic is borrowing a vocabulary that has a body count. That is not a reason to refuse the food. It is a reason to know what the word pure has done, and to stop using it as a compliment.

What the evidence supports, and what it does not

Honest scorecard. The pieces with real independent support are the unglamorous ones: regular meal timing, a larger midday and lighter evening meal, mostly whole and minimally processed food, plenty of plants and legumes, cooking with spices that turn out to be pharmacologically active, and eating slowly without distraction. None of that requires believing anything.

Dosha-specific diets are a different claim, and it is not established. The 2025 review found no validated way to type a person, so a diet prescribed from that typing inherits the problem. The NIH finds little quality evidence for Ayurvedic treatment generally. And the safety findings above concern preparations. The risk lives in the supplements and mineral formulations, not in the dal.

A note on food and restriction
Ayurvedic language can be borrowed easily by disordered eating. “Eat for your type” becomes a rule list; ama becomes a reason to fear food; cleanses, fasting, and detox protocols become permission to restrict, with a spiritual varnish that makes them hard to question. If food is a fraught place for you, the useful parts of this page are the timing, the warmth, and the sitting down, and none of the elimination. Nothing in Ayurveda requires you to earn a meal. If any of this is pulling in that direction, please speak with a clinician, and treat that as the practice rather than an interruption of it.

Evidence, lineage, and contested ground

Three kinds of claim, kept apart

This is the section the rest of the page is built to earn. Ayurveda makes several sorts of claim at once, and most writing about it, friendly and hostile alike, mixes them. Keeping them apart is what lets the good parts survive scrutiny.

The three layers
  1. Physiology. Measurable. Regular sleep and meal timing affect metabolic health. Warm oil massage lowers arousal. These stand or fall on evidence, and mostly they stand.
  2. Tradition. Internal to a lineage. A marma concentrates prana; ojas is the essence of the tissues. Coherent within the system, and beyond the reach of the first layer’s methods in either direction.
  3. History. How the tradition arrived here. This is the layer usually missing, and the one that most changes how you read the other two.

Lineage is part of the validity, and not the whole of it. Charaka and Sushruta record real clinical observation, refined across centuries by practitioners whose reputations and patients depended on it. That transmission is evidence of a kind: a useless idea rarely survives two thousand years of people needing it to work. What lineage cannot do is settle a question of physiology. Age is not proof, and treating it as proof is the move this page is trying not to make.

Where Western science contests it

Researchers have looked seriously for measurable correlates of constitution. Prasher and colleagues found genome-expression differences between extreme prakriti types, which is a real result worth following. The 2025 review of assessment tools nevertheless concluded that no established biomarker for classification exists and that the typing instruments are not yet reliable.

On treatment the NIH is blunt: a few trials suggest benefit in osteoarthritis and type 2 diabetes, “but most of these trials are small or not well-designed,” with “little scientific evidence on Ayurveda’s value for other health issues.”

And the safety finding is not a footnote. A 2015 survey found 40 percent of people taking Ayurvedic preparations had elevated blood lead; a quarter of supplements tested carried high lead and nearly half high mercury.

Where the tradition’s own history contests it

The metals are the clearest case, and the history exonerates most of Ayurveda while indicting one part of it precisely. Lead and mercury come overwhelmingly from rasa shastra, the alchemical tradition of processed minerals. Dagmar Wujastyk’s Indian Alchemy gathers those Sanskrit sources from the ninth to sixteenth centuries and finds them centrally concerned with mercury, framing alchemy as a knowledge system descending from Shiva. That is a distinct, later stream with its own texts and its own gods. “Ayurveda contains heavy metals” and “this medieval alchemical tradition works with processed mercury” are different sentences, and only the second is accurate.

The larger correction is that the Ayurveda most Westerners meet is modern. Its current institutional shape dates to a nineteenth-century revival under British colonial pressure, when practitioners professionalized and partly rebuilt the system in biomedicine’s image in order to survive it. Jean Langford’s Fluent Bodies follows how Ayurveda was remade as a sign of national culture, a remedy for colonial as much as bodily disorder. The claim of an unbroken five-thousand-year science is itself a modern artifact.

Wujastyk and Smith then name the genre this page belongs to. Following Zysk, the marks of what scholars call New Age Ayurveda are: “attributing a remote age to Ayurveda and making it the source of other medical systems,” “linking Ayurveda closely to Indian spirituality, especially Yoga,” “making Ayurveda the basis of mind-body medicine,” and “claiming the ‘scientific’ basis of Ayurveda and its intrinsic safety as a healing modality.”

Read the second one again. A yoga site adding an Ayurveda page is doing the thing being described. Selby documents how “Ayuryoga” became a branded commodity in North American spa culture. Giving Ayurveda its own page rather than dissolving it into the eight limbs is the small structural answer to that, and it is not a full defence. Being named accurately is better than pretending to stand outside the pattern.

The same scholars turn this on a source behind this page. Vasant Lad’s Textbook of Ayurveda calls the doshas “the agents of DNA,” locates prana in the hypothalamus, and compares soma to serotonin. Wujastyk and Smith call these “speculative equations” belonging to a century-long effort to legitimate Ayurveda against European medicine, and note the book is “pedagogical but not historically or textually referenced.” Lad is worth reading. He is a practitioner teaching a living system, which is a real thing to be. He is not a historian, and a dosha is not a strand of DNA.

Ayurveda, not homogenous in itself to begin with, had to contend with British colonialism and the dominance of allopathic medicine, the pressures of modernization, and its own diaspora into the world beyond South Asia. PARAPHRASING WUJASTYK & SMITH, MODERN AND GLOBAL AYURVEDA (SUNY PRESS), INTRODUCTION

None of this is a reason to put the tradition down. It is a reason to hold it the way you would hold any long, living, argued-over body of knowledge: with respect for what it has carried, curiosity about what it got right before anyone could explain why, and clear eyes about the parts that were added later, sold harder, or bent to survive an empire.

Where it actually meets the practice

Four honest connections

Popular writing pairs Ayurveda with all eight limbs, one to one. Some of those pairings are real and some are padding, and the padding tends to be where the herbs are. These four earn their place.

Niyama → dinacharya
The strongest link. Shaucha, tapas, and santosha are abstractions until they are a daily routine. Dinacharya is what the second limb looks like on a Tuesday morning.
Asana → prakriti and vikriti
A practice that settles one body aggravates another. Slow and grounding for vata, cooling and less competitive for pitta, warm and vigorous for kapha. The same argument Bernie Clark makes from bone.
Pranayama → prana
The shared vocabulary is most literal here. Both systems treat breath as life force and agree that fast and shallow disturbs while slow and deep settles.
Anatomy → marma
Ayurveda’s own map of the body: 107 vital points, first written down as a surgeon’s knowledge of where not to be struck. It sits beside the muscle diagram.

The ones left out are left out deliberately. Dharana’s usual Ayurvedic pairing is a sattvic diet plus brahmi and ashwagandha, which is mostly herbs, and herbs are the part of this tradition carrying the safety findings above. Samadhi’s pairing is that Ayurveda “clears the path,” which is not a claim so much as a compliment. Leaving them out is a way of taking the other four seriously.
