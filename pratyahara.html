Pratyahara

pratyāhāra · the fifth limb

Drawing the senses inward to find the threshold where meditation begins

Begin here

The tortoise practice

The Bhagavad Gita gives this limb its lasting image: “One who is able to withdraw his senses from sense objects, as the tortoise draws its limbs within the shell, is firmly fixed in perfect consciousness” (Gita 2.58). The practice below draws the limbs in one at a time.

Practice · sense by sense
Drawing the limbs into the shell
  1. Sit or lie comfortably where you will be undisturbed. Let the breath settle into a slow, even rhythm; a few minutes of pranayama prepares this ground well.
  2. Close the eyes. Sight is the first limb drawn in. Notice how much attention returns the moment the eyes stop reaching.
  3. Listen to the sounds around you. Name none of them. Let each one arrive and pass like weather, until hearing rests without grasping.
  4. Feel the points of contact: the seat, the hands, the air on the skin. Acknowledge each sensation, then release the attention that holds it.
  5. Notice smell and taste, the quietest senses, and let them go last. What remains is awareness resting in its own field, senses present and unentangled.
  6. Rest there for a few minutes. When the mind reaches outward again, and it will, draw the limb back in gently. The returning is the practice.

Withdrawal is a soft word here. The senses keep working; the tortoise keeps its limbs. What changes is the entanglement: attention stops running out along each sense to grip its object. Systematic relaxation, yoga nidra, and savasana at the end of an asana practice all train the same release.

The hinge limb

Where the outer practice turns inward

Patanjali gives pratyahara two sutras, and they are hinges for the whole eight-limbed path. “The disengaging of the powers from entanglement in outer things” is how Charles Johnston renders Yoga Sutra II.54, and II.55 completes the thought: “Thereupon follows perfect mastery over the powers.” The first four limbs work on the visible life: ethics, discipline, posture, breath. At pratyahara the practice crosses a threshold and disappears from view.

The Gheranda Samhita, which arranges its hatha yoga in seven limbs rather than eight, keeps pratyahara at the same hinge: the senses steadied so the mind can be gathered. Whatever the count of limbs, the tradition agrees on the order of operations. Sense-withdrawal comes before concentration because a mind still wired to every sound and itch has nothing left over to concentrate with.

In a 2024 survey of 37 yoga teachers in Delhi, every limb was reported as part of their practice, but the emphasis fell unevenly: pranayama (91.9%) and asana (89.2%) led, while pratyahara (70.3%) sat among the least emphasized limbs. The thinness of this limb in modern teaching is measurable, and it is exactly the gap this page is for.

The map of the inward turn

Roots, ground, branches, and the world beyond

There is a meditation almost every practitioner meets sooner or later. You sit, and you imagine roots growing down from the base of your seat, threading into the earth, anchoring you. Then you imagine light rising up the spine and pouring out through the crown of the head, a column of brightness opening into the sky. In that single image, the whole map is drawn. Once the senses turn inward, the practice reaches in both directions at once: down into what is hidden, up into what is vast.

The World Beyond
Universe · the absolute

The crown opens and the practice expands past the personal entirely, into the absolute realm where nothing is relative. This is the reach of samadhi and sat-chit-ananda.

The Metaphysical
Branches · subtle worlds

Above the dense body lie the subtle and causal sheaths, the symbolic, energetic, dreaming layers the tradition calls the koshas and the realms it calls lokas. Meditation as the discovery of worlds.

The Material World
Ground · the body breathing

The seat, the bones, the breath, the present moment. Meditation is also the discovery of this world, seen at last without the haze of distraction. The ground is where the practice finally arrives.

The Subconscious
Roots · what lies beneath

Roots descend from the seat into the dark and sustaining depths: memory, instinct, the buried patterns that move us before we choose. To meet them in stillness is the beginning of freedom from them.

Notice that the same practice does two opposite things. It is a discovery of other worlds, the subtle planes the tradition names akasha and the lokas, what later esoteric traditions called the astral. And it is a discovery of this world, the one already here, the breath and the body and the ordinary afternoon, which we have simply never been still enough to see. The mystic who travels furthest inward and the practitioner who arrives most fully in the present are describing the same journey from two ends.

And here the cosmic image returns. The roots reaching down and the light reaching up are only a beginner’s compass, a way to orient a mind that still believes in up and down. The further the practice goes, the more those directions dissolve, until what remains is an expansion outward in every direction, the seated body a still point and the awareness around it opening like space without limit. The map is a tree. The territory is a universe.

The measurable neighbor

Interoception: what science can see of the inward turn

Sense-withdrawal has no laboratory instrument, but its nearest measurable neighbor does. Interoception is the perception of the body from within: the heartbeat, the breath, the gut, the felt sense of being a body at all. When attention stops running outward along the five senses, what it meets first is this inner field, and interoception is where contemplative science has learned to look.

The field's consensus roadmap (Khalsa et al. 2018) maps interoception across psychiatric conditions and names it a basic dimension of mental health. A multi-author review (Farb et al. 2015) draws the explicit bridge to contemplative practice: attending inward is trainable, and the training matters for health. The standard measure is a questionnaire, the MAIA (now MAIA-2), which breaks “interoceptive awareness” into scales like noticing, not-distracting, and trusting, close cousins of what pratyahara cultivates.

The evidence is honest rather than tidy. A 2025 meta-analysis of 29 randomized trials found that meditation training reliably improves self-reported interoception. A 2023 yoga study is the counterweight: ten weeks of practice moved no objective measure of cardiac or gastric accuracy. Read together, the research suggests that practice changes the relationship to inner sensation, how it is noticed, trusted, and met, more readily than it sharpens detection itself. The tradition would recognize that distinction: pratyahara cultivates freedom from the pull of what is sensed, and it promised sharper hearing to no one.
