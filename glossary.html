A

Ahimsa

ahimsa

Non-harming: the first yama in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras. Arjuna's refusal to fight is framed initially as ahimsa. The Bhagavad Gita reframes it: the deepest harm can be the refusal of one's own dharma. The soul cannot be killed; grief at action is not the same as grief at harm.

Wisdomlib ↗Yama

Akasha

akasha

Space or ether: the subtlest of the five great elements and the medium of sound. In later esoteric traditions, the "akashic" field of subtle impression. The element through which the metaphysical reaches open.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation

Anatta

anatta / anatman

In Buddhism, not-self: the teaching that no permanent, unchanging Self can be found. Where the Vedic tradition finds atman, the Buddha found a flowing process with no fixed owner. The great divergence between the two streams.

Source ↗Meditation

Aparigraha

aparigraha

Non-grasping, the fifth yama. Holding possessions, people, and outcomes with an open hand.

Wisdomlib ↗Yama

Asana

asana

The third limb: posture, originally the steady and comfortable seat that makes long meditation possible, later the whole physical practice of yoga.

Wisdomlib ↗Asana

Asteya

asteya

Non-stealing, the third yama. Taking nothing that is unoffered: objects, time, credit, energy, or attention.

Wisdomlib ↗Yama

Atman

atman

In the Vedic tradition, the true Self: eternal, unchanging, pure awareness, ultimately one with Brahman, the ground of all being. What meditation uncovers beneath the sheaths.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation

Avatar

avatar

A direct descent of the divine into the material world. The sequence mirrors evolutionary biology: fish, tortoise, boar, half-lion, dwarf, warrior-sage, ideal king, divine human.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree
B

Bhagavad Gita

bhagavad gita

The divine song: 700 verses spoken by Krishna to Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, embedded within the Mahabharata. The central teaching is nishkama karma, act fully, without attachment to outcomes. It is the most-cited scripture in the yoga tradition.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Bhakti

bhakti

Devotional service: the yoga of love. Understood in the Bhagavatam as the highest form of practice because it operates through the heart. Prahlada's story is its primary teaching text.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Bodhisattva

bodhisattva

One who vows to attain full awakening for the sake of all beings, and to keep returning across lifetimes until every being is free. The bodhisattva vow extends into future lives.

Source ↗MeditationUnder the Banyan Tree

Brahmacharya

brahmacharya

Right use of vital energy, the fourth yama. Often rendered as moderation or continence: desire directed rather than scattered.

Wisdomlib ↗Yama

Buddha-nature

tathagatagarbha

The Mahayana teaching that the potential for full awakening is possessed equally by all beings, covered over like the sun behind clouds. Everyone, without exception, can become a Buddha.

Source ↗MeditationUnder the Banyan Tree

Buddhi

buddhi

The discerning intellect; the faculty of judgment and discrimination within the inner instrument. The clear light of buddhi distinguishes the real from the unreal.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation
C

Chakra

cakra

The subtle energy centers along the spine, from root to crown, used on this site to organize pose families and their qualities.

Asana
D

Dana

dana

Generosity, the practice of giving freely without expectation of return. In the yogic tradition, teaching was offered and the gift kept it alive for the next person.

Support

Dharana

dharana

Concentration; one-pointed focus of the mind on a single object such as the breath, a point of light, or a mantra. The sixth limb.

Wisdomlib ↗Dharana

Dharma

dharma

From a root meaning "to uphold." Simultaneously cosmic order, sacred duty, righteousness, and the natural law that holds everything together. Always contextual: the dharma of a king differs from that of a sage.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Dhyana

dhyana

Meditation proper: when concentration becomes effortless and the flow of awareness toward its object is unbroken. The seventh limb. The Pali form, jhana, became the Chinese Chan and the Japanese Zen.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation

Dinacharya

dinacarya

The Ayurvedic daily rhythm: rising, cleansing, eating, moving, and resting in time with the day.

Ayurveda

Dosha

dosha

The three constitutional energies of Ayurveda, vata, pitta, and kapha, read as patterns of movement, heat, and stability in body and mind.

Ayurveda

Drishti

drishti

The yogic gaze: fixed focal points used inside the asanas, turning a physical pose into a moving concentration practice.

Dharana
E

Ekagrata

ekagrata

One-pointedness. The gathered quality of a mind resting on a single object, the fruit of dharana.

Wisdomlib ↗Dharana
G

Guna

guna

The three qualities of manifest existence: tamas (inertia), rajas (activity, passion), and sattva (clarity, harmony). Spiritual practice generally cultivates sattva and uses rajas purposefully.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree
I

Interoception

The perception of the body from within: heartbeat, breath, gut, the felt sense of being a body. Contemporary science’s nearest measurable neighbor to pratyahara.

Introspect

Ishvara pranidhana

Ishvara pranidhana

Surrender to the divine: the fifth niyama in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras. Requires relinquishing both actions and their outcomes. Bali's gift to Vamana is its defining story.

Wisdomlib ↗The Divine
J

Japa

japa

Repetition of a mantra or sacred name, silently or aloud, so a single sound becomes the point the mind holds.

Wisdomlib ↗Dharana

Jhana

jhana

The Buddhist ladder of meditative absorption, climbing rungs nearly identical to the yogic samadhi.

Alchemy
K

Karma

karma

Action, and the principle that every action creates an energy that returns to its source. The quality of intention and the direction of attention both matter.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree
L

Leela

leela

Divine play. The stories of Vishnu's avatars are acts of Leela: play that reveals the nature of spiritual reality. Seeing life as Leela is itself a practice.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Loka

loka

A world or plane of existence. The cosmology describes many lokas, from the dense material world to subtle and celestial realms, the worlds a meditator may discover within.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation
M

Madhurya

madhurya

Sweetness; the intimate quality of Krishna's divine aesthetic. Distinguished from aishwarya (majestic reverence), madhurya is the quality of love that meets God as a beloved friend, child, or lover. The Rasalila is its highest expression. The tradition considers it the most direct path to intimacy with the divine.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Manas

manas

The sensory, processing mind that receives impressions and chatters. One faculty of the inner instrument (antahkarana), distinct from the awareness that witnesses it.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation

Mantra

mantra

Sacred sound. A syllable or phrase repeated (japa) to gather the mind and carry it toward meditation. The tradition holds the sound itself to be a doorway, with Om as the seed-vibration of the absolute.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation

Marma

marman

The vital points of the classical body described in the Sushruta Samhita, mapped alongside the muscles on the muscle diagram.

Wisdomlib ↗Muscle Diagram

Moksha

moksha

Liberation; release from the cycle of suffering and rebirth into the realization of one’s true nature. The fourth and final aim of human life.

Wisdomlib ↗MeditationUnder the Banyan Tree
N

Nada

nada

The inner sound; the subtle vibration heard in deep stillness. Nada yoga is the path of meditation through sound.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation

Nama-japa

nama-japa

Repetition of the divine name. The tradition holds that the name carries the actual presence of the divine. Prahlada's continuous chanting under persecution is its primary teaching text.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Nishkama karma

nishkama karma

Action without attachment to its fruits: the central teaching of the Bhagavad Gita, taught directly by Krishna to Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. Rama's dharmic action across the Ramayana and Bali's gift to Vamana are its living demonstrations.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Niyama

niyama

The second limb: five inner observances, including purity, contentment, self-discipline, self-study, and surrender to the divine. What we mean by the divine →

Wisdomlib ↗The Divine
P

Pancha Kosha

pancha kosha

The five sheaths that cover the Self: the physical, the energetic (breath), the mental, the wisdom, and the bliss bodies. Meditation peels them back, layer by layer, toward the center.

Wisdomlib ↗Meditation

Patanjali

patañjali

The sage credited with compiling the Yoga Sutras, the roughly two-thousand-year-old aphorisms that organize yoga into eight limbs. Temple iconography shows him as half-human, half-serpent, and scholars still debate whether the grammarian and the yoga compiler were the same person.

Wisdomlib ↗YamaAlchemy

Prakriti

prakriti

In Ayurveda, an individual constitution: the personal balance of the doshas a person is born with.

Ayurveda

Pranayama

prana + ayama

The regulation and expansion of life-force (prana) through the breath. The fourth limb of yoga, the bridge between the body and the mind.

Wisdomlib ↗Breath

Pratipaksha bhavana

pratipaksha bhavana

The cultivation of the opposite: a teaching from Patanjali. Transformation of a negative state so complete that what remains is capable of its opposite, reaching past suppression into genuine change.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Pratyahara

pratyahara

Withdrawal of the senses from the outer world, drawing inward as a tortoise draws in its limbs. The fifth limb, and the true threshold of meditation.

Wisdomlib ↗Introspect

Purusharthas

purusharthas

The four goals of human life: dharma (duty), artha (material welfare), kama (desire and love), moksha (liberation). Bali holds dharma first and receives all four.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree
R

Rasa

rasa

Taste or flavor: the specific emotional-spiritual quality of a devotional experience. Receiving a story's rasa is itself a spiritual practice.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Rasalila

rasalila

The circle dance of Krishna with the gopis under the autumn full moon: the highest symbol of devotional yoga in the Bhakti tradition. Krishna stands at the center; the gopis move on the circumference, holding hands. Their longing toward the center is the structure of all devotional practice.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree
S

Samadhi

samadhi

Absorption; the eighth limb, in which the meditator and the object of meditation merge and the separate sense of self dissolves into the absolute.

Wisdomlib ↗Alchemy

Samatva

samatva

Evenness of mind; equanimity. The Gita's working definition of yoga: samatvam yoga uchyate, evenness is yoga (2.48). The same steadiness in success and failure, gain and loss.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Samyama

samyama

Dharana, dhyana, and samadhi flowing together on a single object (Yoga Sutra 3.4). The complete gathering of the inner practice.

Wisdomlib ↗Dharana

Santosha

samtosha

Contentment, the second niyama. The practiced ability to rest satisfied with what is, before anything changes.

The Divine

Sat-Chit-Ananda

sat-cit-ananda

Being, consciousness, bliss: the threefold nature of the absolute in Vedanta. Sat is eternal existence beyond time; chit is awareness fully conscious of all; ananda is boundless joy. This is enlightenment, the world beyond.

Yogapedia ↗Alchemy

Satya

satya

Truthfulness: the second yama. Speaking truthfully, and refusing to live inside a lie even when the lie would be more comfortable. Sita's final act is its deepest expression.

Wisdomlib ↗Yama

Saucha

shauca

Purity, the first niyama. Cleanliness of body, surroundings, food, and thought, so the practice has a clear vessel to work in.

The Divine

Shakti Pitha

shakti pitha

Sacred sites created where Sati's body fell as Shiva carried it in grief. Wherever a piece fell, the earth became sacred. There are 51 such sites across South Asia, honored to this day.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Svadhyaya

svadhyaya

Self-study: the fourth niyama. Includes examination of what we are most intensely drawn to, most afraid of, most in conflict with.

Wisdomlib ↗The Divine

Swadharma

swadharma

One's own specific path of duty, distinct from dharma in general. The Bhagavad Gita holds that it is better to fulfill one's own swadharma imperfectly than another's perfectly. The Mahabharata tests this principle in every character: the question is never only what the right action is, but who we are while doing it.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree
T

Tapas

tapas

Literally heat or fire. The disciplined effort that burns away what is extraneous, refining the practitioner toward clarity. Parvati's tapas to win Shiva back is its defining story.

Wisdomlib ↗The Divine

Trataka

trataka

Steady gazing, classically on a candle flame, until the eyes and mind grow still. A cleansing practice and a doorway into dharana.

Wisdomlib ↗Dharana
U

Ujjayi

ujjayi

The victorious or ocean breath. A gentle narrowing of the throat turns the breath to a soft oceanic sound, the inhale gathering like a wave and the exhale crashing like surf. The carrier-breath of meditation.

Wisdomlib ↗Breath
V

Vaikuntha

vaikuntha

The eternal spiritual realm: the residence of Vishnu. The gate where Jaya and Vijaya stand is the boundary between the eternal and the manifest.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Vinyasa

vinyasa

Movement linked to breath; the connecting flows placed between poses in a sequence.

Viveka

viveka

Discernment: the capacity to distinguish what is real from what is not, what is dharmic from what is not. Vibhishana's defection from Ravana to Rama is its defining story.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree
Y

Yagya

yagya

Fire sacrifice or ceremony. Daksha's exclusion of Shiva and Sati from his yagya, and Bali's great yagya at which Vamana arrives, are both pivotal moments in these stories.

Wisdomlib ↗Under the Banyan Tree

Yama

yama

The first limb: five ethical restraints toward the world, including non-harming (ahimsa) and truthfulness (satya). The roots of the practice.

Wisdomlib ↗Yama

Yantra

yantra

A sacred geometric form used as an object of concentration, its center drawing the attention inward.
