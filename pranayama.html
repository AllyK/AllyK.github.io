Breath

Prāṇa, the life force, and āyāma, its expansion: a searchable field guide to the breath practices

What pranayama is

The deliberate shaping of breath

Pranayama joins two Sanskrit roots: prana, the breath of life, and ayama, expansion or regulation. It is the deliberate modification of breathing, ranging from rapid diaphragmatic breathing to slow, deep breathing, alternate-nostril breathing, and breath retention, most often practiced seated. The tradition names four parts to every round: puraka (inhalation), recaka (exhalation), antah kumbhaka (breath held in), and bahih kumbhaka (breath held out). In Patanjali's Yoga Sutras, pranayama is the fourth limb of the eight-fold path.

A 2020 systematic review in the International Journal of Yoga gathered eighteen controlled trials on pranayama alone and found real, measurable effects: improved pulse rate, blood pressure, and lung function in bronchial asthma; better symptom and activity scores in COPD; reduced fatigue and anxiety in cancer patients; and lower blood pressure in hypertension. The proposed mechanisms are physiological and specific: slow, deep breathing stretches lung tissue in a way that shifts the autonomic nervous system from sympathetic toward parasympathetic dominance, largely through the vagus nerve, while forceful practices like Bhastrika and Kapalabhati train the inspiratory and expiratory muscles directly.

Before you begin

Practice in a well-ventilated room, seated with an erect spine, on a fairly empty stomach. Keep the face and shoulders relaxed. Every technique below can be built slowly: fewer rounds, gentler force, shorter holds, always. If anything produces dizziness, breathlessness, or strain, return to normal breathing and ease off.

Use the search bar and the tags below to find a technique by name, in English or Sanskrit, or by what you need it for: calm, focus, warmth, sleep. Twenty-eight breathing techniques

Not every breath practice below comes from the same place. Some are named in the classical Hatha Yoga texts (the Hatha Yoga Pradipika and Gheranda Samhita); others belong to the broader living yogic tradition, including modern teaching lineages and programs like Sudarshan Kriya Yoga and the Kundalini Yoga of Yogi Bhajan; and several are Western clinical or physiological techniques, some borrowed from pranayama and re-tested in hospitals and labs, others developed independently. Classical Pranayama Text

How to practice

  1. Sit tall in a comfortable cross-legged position or chair, spine long and shoulders soft. Rest the left hand on the left knee in Jnana mudra.
  2. Bring the right hand to Vishnu mudra: fold the index and middle fingers into the palm, leaving the thumb, ring, and little fingers free.
  3. Close the right nostril with the thumb. Inhale slowly and fully through the left nostril.
  4. Close the left nostril with the ring finger, release the thumb, and exhale slowly through the right nostril.
  5. Inhale through the right nostril, then close it with the thumb and exhale through the left. This completes one round.
  6. Continue for 5–10 rounds, keeping the breath smooth and unforced.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The tradition's great balancing breath, evening the flow between the two nadis (ida and pingala) and quieting the mind before meditation. Controlled trials in the systematic review report lower blood pressure and pulse; a 2016 study in Scientific Reports found a single session improved fine-motor and spatial-memory retention, pointing to real gains in attention and cognitive control.

Hand position

Vishnu mudra on the breathing hand: index and middle fingers folded to the palm; thumb closes the right nostril, ring and little fingers close the left. The opposite hand rests in Jnana mudra on the knee.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

If one side is chronically restricted, let the breath be uneven rather than forced equal, since a slower count on the tighter side is still a complete breath. To open a tight nostril, use the free hand to press the outer wall (ala) gently outward rather than closing it. See the deviated septum section below for the visualization variant and other adjustments.

Precautions

Children under 12 and anyone with a heart condition should practice without holding the breath between the inhale and exhale.

Ujjayi · the Victorious Breath
CalmingFocusRespiratory
Classical Pranayama Text
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit with a tall spine, mouth closed, breathing only through the nose.
  2. Gently narrow the back of the throat, as if slightly fogging a mirror or about to whisper a secret.
  3. Inhale slowly through the nose, letting the breath brush the narrowed throat and make a soft, oceanic whisper.
  4. Pause briefly at the top of the inhale without straining.
  5. Exhale slowly through the nose with the same gentle throat constriction, longer than the inhale.
  6. Continue for 5–10 minutes, or sustain it through an asana practice.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The audible throat friction naturally slows and lengthens each breath, exactly the slow-breathing pattern shown to raise vagal (parasympathetic) tone and steady the mind. It gently warms the body, builds concentration, and is the breath most often paired with a moving asana practice to keep attention anchored.

Hand position

Jnana mudra resting on the knees; no nostril work is required.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils stay open throughout, so this is fully accessible regardless of nasal restriction. If nasal breathing is difficult on both sides, the same throat constriction can be practiced with the mouth held slightly open as a bridge technique until the passages clear.

Precautions

Avoid forcing the throat constriction if it causes strain; those with serious cardiac issues or hypertension should practice gently and briefly.

Humming Bee Breath
Bhramari
CalmingSleepFocusBlood Pressure
Classical Pranayama Text
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit comfortably with the eyes closed and the face relaxed.
  2. Raise the hands to the face and gently close the ear flaps (tragus) with the thumbs.
  3. Rest the index fingers lightly on the brow, the middle fingers on the closed eyelids, and let the ring and little fingers rest beside the nose and mouth (Shanmukhi mudra).
  4. Inhale deeply through the nose.
  5. Exhale slowly through the nose while humming like a bee, feeling the vibration in the head and chest.
  6. Repeat for 5–10 rounds.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The humming both lengthens the exhale and vibrates the sinuses, associated with a marked rise in nasal nitric oxide. Studied for lowering blood pressure and heart rate and calming the nervous system, it is a reliable pre-sleep and anti-anxiety practice that quiets mental chatter and draws the senses inward (pratyahara).

Hand position

Shanmukhi mudra, "closing the seven gates": thumbs seal the ears, index fingers on the brow, middle fingers on closed eyes, ring fingers beside the nostrils, little fingers at the corners of the mouth.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

No nostril closure is required, so this technique is fully accessible regardless of nasal restriction. If pressing the ears feels uncomfortable, resting the fingertips near the ear canal without fully sealing it still carries much of the calming effect.

Precautions

Generally considered safe for most practitioners, with no significant precautions reported.

Bellows Breath
Bhastrika
EnergizingWarmingRespiratory
Classical Pranayama Text
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit in a comfortable meditative posture with the spine straight and eyes closed.
  2. Inhale deeply through both nostrils, then exhale forcefully through the nose, pumping the navel toward the spine.
  3. Let the next inhale follow immediately with equal force; keep inhale and exhale equally active and rhythmic.
  4. Perform 10–20 forceful breaths, then take one deep inhale, hold briefly, and release slowly.
  5. Rest with normal breathing before repeating for 2–3 rounds.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Named for the blacksmith's bellows, both inhale and exhale are forceful and equal. It raises metabolic heat and alertness, directly strengthens the inspiratory and expiratory muscles, and is used to shake off sluggishness and generate warmth. A classic morning or pre-practice energizer.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees; beginners sometimes rest the hands lightly on the belly to feel the pumping rhythm.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Because both nostrils stay open, a deviated septum is rarely a barrier here. Slow the pace or shorten the rounds if congestion makes forceful nasal breathing uncomfortable.

Precautions

Avoid with heart conditions, hypertension, pregnancy, vertigo, recent abdominal surgery, spinal conditions, glaucoma, or detached retina. Build up gradually and stop if lightheaded.

Skull-Shining Breath
Kapalabhati
EnergizingDetoxRespiratory
Classical Text (Shatkarma)
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall with the hands resting on the knees, palms down. Take a few natural breaths to settle.
  2. Exhale sharply through the nose by pulling the navel in toward the spine; let the inhale that follows happen passively and naturally.
  3. Continue short, active exhales with passive inhales, keeping the chest still, for one round of 20–30 breaths.
  4. Rest with normal breathing, then repeat for 2–3 rounds, building endurance gradually.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Classed as a cleansing kriya rather than a pure pranayama, "skull-shining breath" is named for the bright, clear feeling it leaves in the head. The rapid active exhales tone the abdominal muscles, clear the respiratory passages, and are studied for improving pulmonary function. Deeply energizing and often used to clear mental fog.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees, or hands lightly on the belly for beginners learning to feel the pumping action.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils stay open, so nasal restriction is rarely a barrier; reduce speed and force if the nose feels irritated or congestion makes the sharp exhale uncomfortable.

Precautions

Avoid during pregnancy or menstruation, and with high or low blood pressure, heart disease, hernia, gastric ulcer, epilepsy, vertigo, migraine, significant nosebleeds, detached retina, glaucoma, stroke history, or recent abdominal surgery. A published case report links forceful practice to a pneumothorax, so build up gradually and stop with any chest discomfort.

Cooling Breath
Sheetali
CoolingCalming
Classical Pranayama Text
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit comfortably and take a few natural breaths.
  2. Curl the tongue lengthwise into a tube and extend it slightly past the lips. (If you can't curl your tongue, a genetic trait not everyone has, use Sitkari instead.)
  3. Inhale slowly through the tongue's tube, feeling the cool air.
  4. Hold briefly, then draw the tongue in, close the mouth, and exhale slowly through the nose.
  5. Repeat for 5–10 rounds.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Air drawn across the wet, curled tongue arrives cooled, which is why the tradition prescribes it for excess heat, thirst, and irritability. It calms the nervous system and is a natural choice on hot days, after vigorous practice, or whenever the body or temper runs hot.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

The inhale is through the mouth, so this works regardless of nostril restriction; if one nostril is blocked, the exhale can be released through the open side alone, or through the mouth instead of the nose.

Precautions

Best avoided in cold weather or with an active cold, cough, congestion, asthma flare, or chronic constipation.

Classical Pranayama Text

How to practice

  1. Sit comfortably, open the lips in a slight smile, and bring the upper and lower teeth lightly together.
  2. Inhale slowly through the gaps between the teeth, making a soft hissing sound.
  3. Hold briefly, then close the mouth and exhale slowly through the nose.
  4. Repeat for 5–10 rounds.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The through-the-teeth cousin of Sheetali, for those who can't roll the tongue. It delivers the same cooling, calming effect, easing heat, thirst, and agitation, and is often chosen for its accessibility.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Like Sheetali, the inhale is through the mouth, making this a reliable substitute for anyone who cannot fully close one nostril, or who cannot curl the tongue for Sheetali.

Precautions

Same cautions as Sheetali: avoid in cold weather, with respiratory congestion, or with chronic constipation.

Right-Nostril Solar Breath
Surya Bhedana
EnergizingWarming
Classical Pranayama Text
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit comfortably and bring the right hand to Vishnu mudra.
  2. Close the left nostril with the ring finger.
  3. Inhale slowly and fully through the right nostril only.
  4. Close the right nostril with the thumb, release the left, and exhale slowly through the left nostril.
  5. This completes one round; repeat for 5–10 rounds.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Right-nostril breathing is traditionally linked to the "solar," heating pingala channel and to greater sympathetic activation and alertness. Used to raise energy, warmth, and drive, a morning or pre-activity breath rather than an evening one.

Hand position

Vishnu mudra, as in Nadi Shodhana, breathing only in the right-in, left-out direction.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

If the right nostril is the restricted side, use the free hand to gently draw the ala outward and widen it, or substitute Bhastrika or Kapalabhati for a similarly warming effect that doesn't require single-nostril closure. See the deviated septum section below for more.

Precautions

Avoid with heart disease, high blood pressure, epilepsy, or fever, and after recent brain, heart, or abdominal surgery, since it raises internal heat.

How to practice

  1. Sit comfortably and bring the right hand to Vishnu mudra.
  2. Close the right nostril with the thumb.
  3. Inhale slowly and fully through the left nostril only.
  4. Close the left nostril with the ring finger, release the thumb, and exhale slowly through the right nostril.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The mirror of Surya Bhedana. Left-nostril breathing is linked to the "lunar," cooling ida channel and to parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) activation. Used to cool, calm, and quiet the system, often in the evening or to ease agitation; the left-nostril and vagal emphasis is also associated with lowering blood pressure.

Hand position

Vishnu mudra, breathing in the left-in, right-out direction.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

If the left nostril is restricted, substitute Sheetali or Sitkari for a comparable cooling effect that inhales through the mouth instead. See the deviated septum section below for more adjustments.

Precautions

Avoid in heavy winter cold, with an active cold or congestion, or with low blood pressure or epilepsy.

Three-Part Breath
Dirga · the Complete Yogic Breath
CalmingFocusRespiratory
Yogic Tradition
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit or lie down comfortably, one hand on the belly and one on the chest.
  2. Inhale slowly and let the breath fill first the belly (feel the lower hand rise), then the lower ribs, then the upper chest: three parts of one continuous inhale.
  3. Exhale in reverse: release from the chest, then the ribs, then draw the navel gently in to empty the belly.
  4. Keep the transitions smooth so the breath feels like a single rising and falling wave.
  5. Continue for 5–10 minutes.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The foundational breath of yoga: it teaches full use of the diaphragm and the whole breathing apparatus, which most people underuse. Calming and grounding, it builds breath awareness and lung capacity and is the base on which the more advanced pranayamas are built.

Hand position

No mudra required; the hands rest on the belly and chest to feel the three-part movement. Later they can rest in Jnana mudra on the knees.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together, so nasal restriction is not a barrier; if both sides are congested, the same three-part wave can be practiced with a soft mouth-breath until the nose clears.

Precautions

Very safe. Simply avoid over-filling to the point of strain; the breath should stay smooth and comfortable.

Jayawardena et al., Int J Yoga 2020 Art of Living Healthline One Medical
Interrupted Breath
Viloma
CalmingFocusRespiratory
Yogic Tradition
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit or lie comfortably and settle into a smooth natural breath.
  2. Inhale in stages: draw in a third of a breath, pause for 2–3 seconds, inhale another third, pause again, then inhale the last third until the lungs are comfortably full.
  3. Exhale smoothly and completely, or, for the exhale variation, release in the same staged, paused way.
  4. Begin with the paused inhale and smooth exhale; later practice the paused exhale, then both together.
  5. Continue for 5–8 rounds.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

"Against the wave," the breath is deliberately interrupted rather than continuous. The staged pauses build fine breath control and lung capacity, lengthen the breath, and focus a busy mind by giving it a precise task. Calming, and often used to prepare for longer retentions.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees, or hands on the belly and ribs to feel each staged filling.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together, so nasal restriction is not a barrier. Keep the pauses short if congestion makes staged breathing feel effortful.

Precautions

Keep the pauses gentle; those with high blood pressure or heart conditions should minimize or skip the held segments. Stop if lightheaded.

Equal-Ratio Breath
Sama Vritti
CalmingBalanceFocus
Yogic Tradition
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall and comfortable, breathing through the nose.
  2. Inhale for a count of four.
  3. Exhale for a count of four, matching the inhale exactly.
  4. Keep the two halves even and unforced; as it becomes easy, lengthen both to a count of five or six.
  5. Continue for 5–10 minutes.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

"Equal fluctuation," matching inhale to exhale. The evenness steadies the mind and balances the nervous system, making it a favorite preparation for meditation and a simple anchor in anxious moments. It is the yogic sibling of the Western "box breathing" further down this directory.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees; no nostril work required.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together, so nasal restriction is not a barrier. If airflow is uneven, keep the counts even by feel rather than by force.

Precautions

Very safe. Simply keep the counts comfortable and don't strain to hold a long ratio.

External Retention Breath
Bahya · Bahya Kumbhaka
DigestionFocusCalming
Classical Pranayama Text
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall with the spine erect and hands resting on the knees.
  2. Inhale fully, then exhale completely through the nose, emptying the lungs.
  3. Hold the breath out (external retention). Traditionally this is combined with three locks: draw the chin toward the chest (jalandhara bandha), lift the pelvic floor (mula bandha), and draw the navel up and back toward the spine (uddiyana bandha).
  4. Hold comfortably, then release the locks, lift the chin, and inhale slowly.
  5. Repeat for 3–5 rounds, keeping every hold within easy reach.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

An advanced retention practice. The external hold with abdominal lock massages and tones the abdominal organs, is traditionally used to support digestion and core energy, and demands and builds sharp inward focus. Calming once mastered, it is usually learned after the gentler breaths are steady.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees; the "hand positions" that matter here are the three bandhas (throat, belly, pelvic floor) rather than a finger mudra.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used and the challenge is the breath-out hold, not airflow, so nasal restriction is not a barrier. Shorten the retention if congestion makes the following inhale feel urgent.

Precautions

Avoid the retention and abdominal lock during pregnancy or menstruation, and with heart disease, high blood pressure, hernia, ulcer, or glaucoma. Learn the bandhas with an experienced teacher.

Sources
Jayawardena et al., Int J Yoga 2020 Art of Living Diva Yoga
Swooning Breath
Murcha
FocusCalming
Classical Pranayama Text
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit in a stable, comfortable posture with the spine erect.
  2. Inhale slowly and fully through both nostrils.
  3. Retain the breath in and gently draw the chin toward the chest (jalandhara bandha), letting the gaze soften or the eyes turn slightly upward.
  4. Hold only as long as it stays comfortable, then slowly lift the chin and exhale in a long, smooth stream.
  5. Practice just 3–5 rounds, resting between them.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Named for the light, swooning, dreamlike stillness it can produce. An advanced retention practice used to induce a deeply calm, meditative, inward state and to quiet mental activity almost completely. Approached gently and briefly, it is a doorway toward meditation.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees, with jalandhara bandha (chin lock) as the defining "position." No nostril work required.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together, so nasal restriction is not a barrier; keep the retention brief if congestion makes the hold feel pressured.

Precautions

Because it can produce lightheadedness by design, practice seated, never standing, and stop well before any real dizziness. Avoid with low or high blood pressure, heart conditions, epilepsy, or during pregnancy; best learned with a teacher.

Floating Breath
Plavini
Focus
Classical Pranayama Text

How to practice

  1. This is an advanced, rarely taught practice; approach it only under experienced guidance.
  2. Sit tall and take a few settling breaths.
  3. Fill the lungs completely with a slow, full inhale, and, in the classical description, draw additional air into the stomach so the whole torso feels buoyant and full.
  4. Retain the breath comfortably, feeling the sense of lightness.
  5. Release slowly and completely, then rest before any repetition.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The classical texts describe Plavini as producing such buoyancy that the practitioner could float on water. In practical terms it is a mastery-level retention practice for cultivating deep breath control and one-pointed focus. Included here for completeness; it is not a beginner's breath.

Hand position

Jnana mudra on the knees; no nostril work required.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together, so nasal restriction is not a barrier; the practice's difficulty lies in the retention rather than in airflow.

Precautions

Advanced and specialized. Not for beginners, and not to be self-taught. Avoid entirely with any cardiac, respiratory, digestive, or blood-pressure condition, and during pregnancy. Learn only from an experienced teacher.

OM Chanting
Pranava · Omkara Japa
CalmingFocusSleepBlood Pressure
Yogic Tradition
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall and comfortable with the eyes closed and the face soft.
  2. Inhale slowly and fully through the nose.
  3. On the exhale, chant "OM" (A-U-M), letting the sound stretch across the whole out-breath: the open "aaah," the rounding "ooo," and the humming "mmm" that lingers.
  4. Feel the vibration move through the chest, throat, and head. Let each inhale arrive naturally before the next chant.
  5. Continue for 5–15 minutes, letting the breath rate slow of its own accord.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Chanting naturally stretches the exhale and slows breathing toward the ~6-breaths-per-minute range. Clinical work cited in the pranayama literature (including Italian studies on mantra recitation) links this slowing to increased vagal activity and greater nitric oxide release, with calmer heart rate and blood pressure. Deeply centering, it prepares the mind for meditation and eases the way toward sleep.

Hand position

Jnana or Chin mudra resting on the knees; no nostril work required.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

The inhale can be nasal or, if both sides are congested, a soft mouth-breath; the chant itself is on the exhale, so nasal restriction is not a barrier.

Precautions

Very safe. Simply let the breath stay comfortable and don't force the length of the chant.

Sudarshan Kriya (SKY Breath)
Sudarshan Kriya Yoga
CalmingEnergizingFocusBalance
Yogic Tradition (modern lineage)
 ▾

How to practice

  1. SKY is a structured sequence taught in person by certified Art of Living instructors; the full rhythmic kriya is learned under guidance rather than from text.
  2. It typically strings together several breaths in order: Ujjayi (slow victorious breath), Bhastrika (bellows breath), and the signature cyclical breathing done in slow, medium, and fast rhythms.
  3. The rhythmic portion moves through cycles of breath at varying speeds, guided by count or recording.
  4. A session usually closes with a period of rest and stillness.
  5. Once learned, it is practiced daily at home; a shorter guided version is often used for maintenance.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

A modern, well-studied rhythmic breathing program. Peer-reviewed reviews report benefits for depression, anxiety, stress, and post-traumatic stress, along with improved wellbeing and antioxidant and immune measures. It is used both to calm and to energize, balancing the system through its slow-medium-fast structure.

Hand position

Hands rest in Jnana mudra on the knees for the seated portions; no single-nostril work is involved in the core cyclical breathing.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

The core practice uses both nostrils together, so nasal restriction is generally not a barrier. Mention any significant obstruction, or cardiac or respiratory conditions, to your instructor so pace and force can be adjusted.

Precautions

Best learned in the structured course rather than improvised. Those with cardiac conditions, uncontrolled blood pressure, epilepsy, or during pregnancy should tell the instructor and practice gently.

Diaphragmatic (Belly) Breathing
Clinical · "calming breath" (NHS)
CalmingRespiratoryBlood Pressure
Western Medicine
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit or lie somewhere comfortable. Let the breath flow in through the nose and out through the mouth, without forcing it.
  2. Rest one hand on the chest and one on the belly.
  3. Breathe in gently through the nose, counting steadily to five if you can, and feel the belly (not the chest) rise under the lower hand.
  4. Let the breath flow out slowly through the mouth, again to a count of five.
  5. Keep this soft, steady rhythm for 3–5 minutes.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The core clinical relaxation breath, recommended by the NHS for stress, anxiety, and panic. Slow diaphragmatic breathing engages the parasympathetic nervous system, easing the body out of the fight-or-flight response. It shares its mechanics with the yogic Dirga breath but comes from Western clinical practice; it is the simplest first breath to teach anyone in distress.

Hand position

No mudra. One hand on the chest, one on the belly, purely to feel the diaphragm doing the work rather than the upper chest.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

The NHS version inhales through the nose and exhales through the mouth; if the nose is fully congested, a gentle mouth-inhale still works. Nasal restriction is not a barrier.

Precautions

Very safe for nearly everyone. If slow breathing ever brings on anxiety or breathlessness, return to your natural breath.

Pursed-Lip Breathing
Clinical / respiratory therapy
RespiratoryCalming
Western Medicine
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Relax the neck and shoulders and sit comfortably.
  2. Breathe in slowly through the nose for about two counts, keeping the mouth closed.
  3. Purse the lips as if about to whistle or gently blow out a candle.
  4. Breathe out slowly and steadily through the pursed lips for about four counts, roughly twice as long as the inhale.
  5. Repeat until the breath feels easier, especially during exertion or breathlessness.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

A cornerstone of pulmonary rehabilitation. The back-pressure from pursed lips keeps the airways open longer, slows the breathing rate, and helps empty stale air from the lungs, easing the breathlessness of COPD, asthma, and anxiety. It gives a person a concrete tool to regain control of the breath during exertion or a panic surge.

Hand position

No mudra; the "position" is the pursed lips that create gentle back-pressure on the exhale.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

If the nose is congested, the inhale can also be taken gently through slightly parted lips; the defining feature is the slow pursed-lip exhale, so nasal restriction is not a barrier.

Precautions

Very safe. Those with significant lung disease should learn it as part of a rehab program so it can be paired with activity pacing.

Coherent / Resonance Breathing
Clinical · ~5–6 breaths per minute
CalmingBalanceBlood Pressure
Western Medicine
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit or lie comfortably and breathe through the nose.
  2. Inhale smoothly for a count of about five or six seconds.
  3. Exhale just as smoothly for the same five or six seconds, with no pause or hold at either end.
  4. This gives roughly five to six breaths per minute, the "resonance" rate at which heart rate and breath synchronize.
  5. Continue for 10–20 minutes; many people use a visual or audio pacer to hold the rhythm.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Slowing to around six breaths per minute maximizes heart-rate variability and strengthens the baroreflex, the system that regulates blood pressure. Well studied for lowering blood pressure, easing stress and anxiety, and producing a balanced, coherent physiological state. It is essentially the Western, instrument-measured formalization of the slow breathing the yogic tradition arrived at through chanting and Ujjayi.

Hand position

No mudra; hands rest wherever comfortable. The focus is entirely on the even five-to-six-second pace.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together and the breath is slow and soft, so nasal restriction is rarely a barrier; a gentle mouth-breath can be used if both sides are congested.

Precautions

Very safe. If the slow pace feels like air hunger at first, lengthen the count gradually over several sessions.

Sources
Zaccaro et al., Front Hum Neurosci 2018 (slow breathing review) Russo et al., Breathe 2017 (physiology of slow breathing) Healthline
Cyclic Sighing (Physiological Sigh)
Clinical · double-inhale, long exhale
Calming
Western Medicine
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit or lie comfortably and breathe normally to begin.
  2. Inhale slowly through the nose until the lungs feel about full.
  3. Without exhaling, take a second, short, sharp sip of air through the nose to top the lungs off completely (the "double inhale").
  4. Then exhale slowly and fully through the mouth, letting the out-breath be longer than the whole inhale.
  5. Repeat this cycle for about five minutes.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The physiological sigh is the body's own reset breath (we do it spontaneously when crying or falling asleep); the second inhale reopens collapsed air sacs and the long exhale offloads carbon dioxide. A 2023 Stanford randomized study found five minutes a day of cyclic sighing improved mood and lowered respiratory rate more than mindfulness meditation or other breathwork, making it one of the fastest-acting calming breaths available.

Hand position

No mudra; hands rest comfortably. The defining feature is the double-inhale followed by an extended exhale.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

If a nostril is blocked, the double inhale can be taken through the mouth; the extended exhale is already through the mouth, so nasal restriction is not a barrier.

Precautions

Very safe. Simply keep the double inhale gentle rather than gasping.

Sources
Balban et al., Cell Reports Medicine 2023 (Stanford, cyclic sighing) Fincham et al., 2023 (breathwork meta-analysis) One Medical
4-7-8 Breath
"Relaxing Breath" · pranayama-derived
CalmingSleep
Yogic TraditionWestern Medicine
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit or lie comfortably. Rest the tip of the tongue against the ridge behind the upper front teeth and keep it there throughout.
  2. Exhale completely through the mouth around the tongue, making a soft whoosh.
  3. Close the mouth and inhale quietly through the nose for a count of four.
  4. Hold the breath for a count of seven.
  5. Exhale completely through the mouth, whoosh, for a count of eight. This is one round.
  6. Repeat for four rounds to start, building up slowly over time.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Popularized by Dr. Andrew Weil and adapted from yogic pranayama, this fixed 4-7-8 ratio emphasizes a long exhale and a breath-hold to tip the nervous system toward calm. Widely used as a wind-down and sleep aid and to interrupt anxiety or cravings. It sits between traditions: a yogic-derived retention breath repackaged in Western clinical wellness.

Hand position

No mudra; the only "position" is the tongue resting behind the upper teeth. Hands rest comfortably.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

The inhale is nasal but brief (four counts); if the nose is fully congested, a soft mouth-inhale can bridge it. The long exhale is through the mouth, so nasal restriction is largely not a barrier.

Precautions

The 7-count hold can cause lightheadedness at first; practice seated or lying down and shorten the counts (keeping the 4:7:8 proportion) if needed. Those with low blood pressure, heart conditions, or pregnancy should keep the hold brief.

Sources
Jayawardena et al., Int J Yoga 2020 Healthline One Medical Ohio State Health
Box Breathing
Square / Tactical Breathing
CalmingBalanceFocus
Western Medicine
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit upright and comfortable, breathing through the nose.
  2. Inhale for a count of four.
  3. Hold the breath in for a count of four.
  4. Exhale for a count of four.
  5. Hold the breath out for a count of four. That completes one "box."
  6. Repeat for 4–5 rounds, or a few minutes, keeping all four sides equal.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Four equal sides: in, hold, out, hold. The structure gives an anxious or overloaded mind a simple, absorbing pattern to follow, quickly steadying focus and calming the stress response; it's a staple of military and first-responder training for staying composed under pressure. It is the Western, retention-added cousin of the yogic Sama Vritti.

Hand position

No mudra; this is a tactical and clinical breathing drill, popularized by the military and by first responders, and studied directly in stress research. Hands rest comfortably.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used throughout, and the exhale can move through the nose or mouth, so nasal restriction of any kind is not a barrier.

Precautions

Shorten the counts or drop the holds if lightheaded; those with respiratory or cardiac conditions should keep holds brief.

Sources
Fincham et al., 2023 (breathwork meta-analysis) Balban et al., Cell Reports Medicine 2023 (box-breathing arm) One Medical Ohio State Health
Breath of Fire
Agni Pranayam · Kundalini
EnergizingDetoxFocusRespiratory
Kundalini (Yogi Bhajan)
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall with a long spine, hands resting on the knees, eyes lightly closed.
  2. Breathe through the nose with a rapid, continuous, rhythmic breath in which the inhale and exhale are equal, with no pause between them.
  3. Power the breath from the navel: snap the navel point in toward the spine on each exhale, and let it release for each inhale. The chest stays relaxed and still.
  4. Aim for a steady pace of roughly 2–3 breaths per second once it is familiar; the rhythm is even, like a machine, not gasping.
  5. Begin with 1–3 minutes and build gradually. To finish, inhale deeply, hold briefly, and exhale to settle.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The signature breath of Kundalini Yoga, used in nearly every kriya to build energy, focus, and heat and to "clear" the system. It resembles Kapalabhati but is defined by an equal active inhale and exhale rather than passive inhalation. Kundalini Yoga programs built around Breath of Fire and related pranayam have peer-reviewed support for reducing generalized anxiety (a randomized trial in JAMA Psychiatry), lowering salivary cortisol and perceived stress, and improving cognition and executive function.

Hand position

Jnana or Gyan mudra (thumb and index fingertip together) resting on the knees; beginners may rest a hand on the navel to feel the pumping point.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils stay open, so a deviated septum is rarely a barrier. Slow the pace or shorten the time if congestion makes the rapid nasal breath uncomfortable, and gently widen a tight nostril by drawing its outer wall (ala) outward with a fingertip.

Precautions

Avoid during pregnancy and menstruation, and with heart disease, high blood pressure, vertigo, epilepsy, hernia, gastric ulcer, or recent abdominal surgery. Build up slowly and stop if lightheaded.

Sources
Simon et al., JAMA Psychiatry 2021 (Kundalini yoga for GAD, RCT) Hoge et al., Psychiatry Research 2023 (KY anxiety & depression) Giridharan et al., Cureus 2024 (KY cognition, systematic review) 3HO (Kundalini teaching source)
Long Deep Breathing
Kundalini · complete breath
CalmingRespiratoryBlood PressureFocus
Kundalini (Yogi Bhajan)
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall or lie down, one hand resting on the belly.
  2. Inhale slowly through the nose in one continuous flow: fill the belly first, then expand the ribs, then the upper chest.
  3. Exhale slowly through the nose in reverse: release the chest, then the ribs, then draw the navel in to empty the belly completely.
  4. Keep both the inhale and exhale long, smooth, and unbroken, as slow as is comfortable.
  5. Continue for 3–11 minutes; in Kundalini practice, slowing toward a few breaths per minute is the aim.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

The calming counterpart to Breath of Fire and the first breath taught in Kundalini Yoga. Using the full lung capacity engages the parasympathetic nervous system, settling stress and anxiety and supporting healthy blood pressure. It is a central component of the Kundalini protocols shown in randomized trials to ease generalized anxiety and to lower salivary cortisol and perceived stress.

Hand position

Gyan mudra (thumb and index fingertip together) on the knees, or one hand on the belly and one on the chest to feel the three-part fill.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together, so nasal restriction is not a barrier; if both sides are congested, a soft, slow mouth-breath can bridge it until the passages clear.

Precautions

Very safe. Simply avoid over-filling to the point of strain; the breath should stay smooth and comfortable throughout.

Sources
Simon et al., JAMA Psychiatry 2021 (Kundalini yoga for GAD, RCT) García-Sesnich et al., Int J Yoga 2017 (KY, cortisol & stress) 3HO (Kundalini teaching source)
Left-Nostril Breathing
Kundalini · Ida / calming
CalmingSleepBlood PressureCooling
Kundalini (Yogi Bhajan)
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall and comfortable. Use the right hand to gently close the right nostril: rest the thumb against it, fingers pointing up.
  2. Let the left hand rest in Gyan mudra on the knee.
  3. Breathe long, deep, and slow through the left nostril only, both the inhale and exhale passing through the left side.
  4. Keep the breath smooth and full, without force.
  5. Continue for 3–11 minutes, or 26 slow breaths, especially in the evening or to unwind.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

Kundalini Yoga uses single-nostril breathing to steer the nervous system: breathing through the left nostril (the ida, "moon" channel) is used to cool, calm, and relax, drawing on parasympathetic activation. Unlike the alternating Chandra Bhedana, here the breath stays on the left throughout. It is a simple, portable tool for winding down, easing anxiety, and preparing for sleep, and belongs to the Kundalini protocols studied for anxiety and stress reduction.

Hand position

Right thumb closes the right nostril (fingers extended upward); left hand in Gyan mudra on the knee.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

This depends on the left nostril being open. If the left side is the restricted one, gently draw its outer wall (ala) outward with a fingertip to widen it, warm the passage first with a saline rinse or warm shower, or use the visualization variant, breathing through both nostrils while directing attention to the left. See the deviated septum section below.

Precautions

Very gentle and widely accessible. Those with very low blood pressure or active congestion may prefer a shorter practice; avoid straining a blocked nostril.

Sources
Simon et al., JAMA Psychiatry 2021 (Kundalini yoga for GAD, RCT) García-Sesnich et al., Int J Yoga 2017 (KY, cortisol & stress) 3HO (Kundalini teaching source)
One-Minute Breath
Kundalini · extended breath
CalmingFocusBalance
Kundalini (Yogi Bhajan)
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall and settle with a few natural breaths. This is an advanced practice; build toward it gradually.
  2. Inhale slowly and smoothly through the nose for a count of 20 seconds.
  3. Hold the breath in for 20 seconds.
  4. Exhale slowly and smoothly for 20 seconds. One full cycle spans about one minute.
  5. Begin with far shorter counts (for example 5-5-5 or 10-10-10) and lengthen over weeks. Never strain.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

A Kundalini mastery breath that stretches one full round to roughly a minute. Slowing the breath this far deeply calms the nervous system, sharpens focus, and balances the mind, and it is used to develop steadiness and meditative depth. It embodies the slow-breathing principle (around one breath per minute) that the broader research links to strong parasympathetic and heart-rate-variability effects; approach it patiently over time.

Hand position

Gyan mudra resting on the knees; no nostril work required.

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Both nostrils are used together and the breath is slow and soft, so nasal restriction is rarely a barrier; keep the counts shorter if congestion makes the long, held breath feel pressured.

Precautions

Because of the long retention, build up slowly and practice seated. Those with high or low blood pressure, heart conditions, epilepsy, or during pregnancy should shorten or skip the hold and keep the breath comfortable.

Sources
Zaccaro et al., Front Hum Neurosci 2018 (slow breathing review) Simon et al., JAMA Psychiatry 2021 (Kundalini yoga for GAD, RCT) 3HO (Kundalini teaching source)
Kirtan Kriya (Sa Ta Na Ma)
Kundalini · mantra-breath meditation
FocusCalmingCognitive
Kundalini (Yogi Bhajan)
 ▾

How to practice

  1. Sit tall with the eyes closed. On each breath you will chant the four sounds Saa, Taa, Naa, Maa, letting the exhale carry the sound and a natural inhale return between repetitions.
  2. With each sound, press a fingertip to the thumb in turn: Saa to the index; Taa to the middle; Naa to the ring; Maa to the little finger.
  3. Cycle the volume in three stages: chant aloud for about two minutes, then in a whisper for two minutes, then silently (hearing the sounds inwardly) for four minutes.
  4. Reverse back out: whisper for two minutes, then aloud for two minutes.
  5. A full practice runs about twelve minutes; end with a deep inhale, a moment of stillness, and release.

Benefit & purpose

Why practice it

A widely studied Kundalini meditation that pairs rhythmic breath and chant with finger movement. In a randomized controlled trial in older adults with mild cognitive impairment, a Kundalini program using Kirtan Kriya improved memory and executive function and eased depressive symptoms and boosted resilience more than standard memory training. It is used to sharpen focus and memory while calming the mind, making it a gentle daily practice for cognitive and emotional wellbeing.

Hand position

The mudra is the practice: on each sound, the thumb touches index, then middle, then ring, then little finger, in sequence (Gyan, Shuni, Surya, and Buddhi mudras in turn).

Adaptation for restricted or uneven nostril breathing

Breathing is natural and the sound rides the exhale, so nasal restriction is not a barrier; if the nose is fully congested, let the inhale come softly through the mouth between repetitions.

Precautions

Very safe and gentle. There are no significant physical precautions; simply keep the voice and breath relaxed.

Sources
Eyre et al., Int Psychogeriatrics 2017 (KY & Kirtan Kriya in MCI, RCT) Giridharan et al., Cureus 2024 (KY cognition, systematic review) 3HO (Kundalini teaching source)
Hand positions

The mudras used in these techniques

Three hand positions recur through the directory above. Each card names which one it uses.

Jnana / Chin Mudra

The palm faces up; the pad of the index finger folds in to touch the thumb tip in a soft circle (gold), while the middle, ring, and little fingers stay long and relaxed. The back of the hand rests on the knee for most seated techniques.

Vishnu Mudra

The index and middle fingers fold fully into the palm (the two short stubs). The thumb (gold) stays free to close the right nostril; the ring and little fingers (teal) stay free to close the left. Used for every alternate- and single-nostril technique in the directory.

Shanmukhi Mudra

"Closing the seven gates," both hands on the face: thumbs seal the ears (gold circles), index fingers rest on the brow, middle fingers on the closed eyes, ring fingers beside the nostrils, little fingers at the corners of the mouth. The gold dots mark where each fingertip lands. Used for Bhramari.

Practicing with a deviated septum or nasal restriction

When one side doesn't open the way the other does

Many people breathe more easily through one nostril than the other, whether from a deviated septum, chronic congestion, or the nasal cycle everyone experiences (the natural, hours-long alternation in which one nostril is dominant while the other rests). Nostril-specific pranayama can still be practiced with a few adjustments.

Adaptations
  • Open, don't force. Instead of pressing a finger fully across the blocked nostril, use the opposite hand's finger to gently press against the outer wall (the ala) of the restricted nostril, widening the airway rather than closing it further.
  • Let the ratio be uneven. If air moves more slowly on one side, allow that side a longer count rather than straining to match the open side. The point is a full, unforced breath, not a symmetrical clock.
  • Try the visualization variant. When one nostril cannot be closed or opened at will, breathe naturally through both and simply direct attention to the left side, then the right, in the same rhythm the physical practice would use. Several teachers and clinics describe this mental alternate-nostril breath as carrying much of the calming, focusing effect even without full physical closure.
  • Warm the passage first. A warm shower, a few rounds of gentle Ujjayi, or a saline rinse beforehand can temporarily ease congestion enough to practice normally.
  • Swap in a two-nostril technique. Ujjayi, Bhastrika, Kapalabhati, Dirga, Viloma, Sama Vritti, and Bahya all breathe through both nostrils at once and require no single-nostril closure, making them reliable substitutes for Nadi Shodhana, Surya Bhedana, or Chandra Bhedana on days when one side won't cooperate.
  • Use a mouth-inhale technique for cooling work. Sheetali and Sitkari inhale through the mouth, so they deliver a cooling breath even when nasal breathing is difficult on both sides.

A structural deviated septum that causes significant, chronic obstruction is worth mentioning to an ENT or your physician, particularly before attempting forceful practices like Bhastrika or Kapalabhati on the restricted side.
