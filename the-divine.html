The Divine

Oneness, and the reciprocity that binds all things, living and not

Niyama · Ishvara pranidhana

What we mean by the divine

The word divine arrives carrying many pictures. In some traditions it means a creator, a personal presence, a being who makes and watches the world. Those images are ancient and dear, and they have their place. In this living practice we hold the divine first in another way, one the yoga traditions have carried just as long.

The divine, here

In this practice, the divine is oneness: the single wholeness in which everything participates, the ground that every separate thing is a face of.

And the divine is reciprocity: the living give-and-take that binds all things, living and not, into one belonging. To touch the divine is to feel that nothing stands alone, that you are made of everything and everything is made, in part, of you.

You do not have to believe in a god to meet the divine in this sense. You only have to notice, clearly, that you inter-are with all that is.

Two great streams of the tradition carry this understanding. The first looks inward and finds that the many are secretly one. The second looks outward and finds that the one lives as endless mutual relationship. They meet in the middle, and the practice is how we learn to feel them.

The first stream · non-duality

The many are one: Advaita

The Upanishads, the contemplative heart of the Vedas, return again and again to a single discovery: behind the countless forms of the world there is one reality, Brahman, and that reality is also the innermost Self, Atman, in every being. The divine is the wholeness, and you are not outside it.

Īśāvāsyam idaṃ sarvam
All this, whatever moves in this moving world, is pervaded by the divine. The opening words of the Isha Upanishad: there is no place the sacred is absent.
Isha Upanishad, verse 1
Sarvaṃ khalvidaṃ brahma
All this is indeed Brahman. The world and its source are one reality seen in two ways.
Chandogya Upanishad 3.14.1
Tat tvam asi
Thou art That. The wholeness you seek is the very awareness that is seeking. The drop is told, gently, that it has always been the ocean.
Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.7, one of the four mahavakyas

This is Advaita, not-two. The divine is the seamless being, consciousness, and bliss that the meditation tradition calls sat-chit-ananda. Realizing it is the aim of contemplative yoga: to know directly that the witness in you and the ground of all things are one.

The second stream · reciprocity

The one lives as relationship: interbeing

If the first stream looks inward to find the one, the second looks outward and finds that the one is made entirely of relationship. Nothing exists by itself. Everything is woven of everything else, and that endless mutual belonging is itself the face of the divine.

Vedic

Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam

"The world is one family." A line from the Maha Upanishad, understood to embrace all people and all life alike: animals, plants, the whole living earth, one household.

Buddhist

Interbeing

Thich Nhat Hanh’s word for dependent co-arising: to be is to inter-be. In a sheet of paper he saw the cloud, the rain, the tree, the sun. Nothing can be, by itself alone.

Indra’s Net

A net stretching infinitely in all directions, a jewel at every knot, and each jewel reflecting every other. Touch one, and the whole net trembles. Each thing contains all things.

Living earth

Reciprocity

The give-and-take that sustains all life: breath traded with trees, water with clouds, nourishment with soil. To receive and to give back, in balance, is to live the divine as practice.

Plum Village: Interbeing ↗

In this view the divine is not far away and not only within. It is the relationship itself, the breathing exchange between you and the tree, between the rain and the river, between one being and the next. Reverence becomes reciprocity: to honor the divine is to give back to the web that holds you.

The divine in practice

How this lives on the mat

This is the second limb, Niyama, and its final observance is Ishvara pranidhana, surrender to the divine. Read in this way, surrender is the practice of remembering oneness and acting from reciprocity. It threads through the whole of yoga.

In the Yamas, the ethical roots, reciprocity becomes action: ahimsa, non-harming, is the simplest acknowledgment that another’s life is woven with your own. In Asana, the body learns that it is made of breath traded with the world. In meditation, the practitioner rests in the wholeness directly, until the boundary between self and all-that-is grows quiet.

Surrender to the divine, then, asks very little belief and a great deal of attention. It asks that you notice, in the pose and in the breath and in the meeting with another being, that you were never separate. The remembering is the worship.

Where the streams meet

Non-duality says the many are one. Reciprocity says the one lives as the loving relationship of the many. Held together, they describe a single truth from two directions: you are the whole, and you belong to it. The divine is both the ocean and the trading of the waves.

1.Where in an ordinary day do you most clearly feel that you inter-are with the world, that you are made of things outside yourself?
2.If the divine is reciprocity, what are you receiving without yet giving back, and what might balance look like?
3.Tat tvam asi: thou art That. Sitting quietly, can you sense the awareness in you as the same awareness that lights all things?
