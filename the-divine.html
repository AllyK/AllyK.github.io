The Divine

Oneness, and the reciprocity that binds all things, living and not

A practice, before any of the theory

Take refuge, in your own language

Tara Brach teaches the Buddhist practice of taking refuge in a way that makes room for almost anyone. The first refuge, she writes, “may be approached on various levels, and we can choose the way most meaningful to our particular temperament.” She sometimes says “I take refuge in the Beloved.” You might say something else. Put your word in and read the three lines slowly.

I take refuge in the Beloved,
I take refuge in the dharma, the way things actually are,
I take refuge in the sangha, the ones practicing alongside me.
Nothing is stored or sent. The words stay on your screen. Brach’s point is that the refuge is real whichever door you come through: “not trying to get rid of fear, but rather letting go into a refuge that is vast enough to hold my fear with love.”
Three doorways

Choose the way in that is honest for you

People arrive at this word from different places, and the practice does not require you to pretend. Pick the door that fits, and start there. You can come back for the others.

A presence that hears
You believe in, or are drawn toward, a personal divine: God, Christ, Krishna, the Divine Mother. Devotion is the practice, and surrender is real surrender to someone.
Energies and archetypes
The gods are essences to learn from rather than beings to petition. Shiva is what dissolution feels like; Kali is rage that serves love. This is where I stand.
The oneness of all things
No creator, no petition, and still something worth the word sacred: the seamless whole, and the reciprocity that binds every part of it to every other.

Positionality

This is the hardest one for me

A note from Ally

Of the eight limbs, this is the one I understand least, and I would rather say that plainly than write around it. Every other page here I can ground in anatomy, in a text, in a study. This one asks me what I believe, and my honest answer is still moving.

Where I am writing from: I am an American woman, raised Catholic, and I do not identify as a theist, at least not in the creator-god sense. That upbringing gave me a vocabulary of reverence and a deep suspicion of certainty, and I carry both. So when I meet the gods of yoga I do not meet them as beings who made the world and watch it. I understand them as energetic stories: essences and archetypes to learn from. Kali is what it is like when rage serves love. Shiva is what dissolution feels like from the inside. Hanuman is devotion that does not calculate. These are true in the way that a story is true, which is not a lesser kind of true.

That is my reading, and it is only mine. Some practitioners find the divine exactly where the texts put it, as a literal presence who hears them, and their practice is not naive; it is a relationship, and it works. Others find it where I tend to, in the oneness of all things and the reciprocity that runs between them. The tradition is old enough and large enough to hold all of us, and this page is written so that none of those readers has to convert to keep reading.

What I have stopped doing is arguing about it in my own head while I am on the mat. The instruction that follows, from Tara Brach, is most of the reason why.

Niyama · Ishvara pranidhana

What we mean by the divine

The word divine carries many pictures. In some traditions it names a creator, a personal presence who makes and watches the world. Those images are old and they have their place. This practice holds the divine first in another way, one the yoga traditions have carried just as long: as oneness, and as the reciprocity that binds all things.

The divine, here

In this practice, the divine is oneness: the single wholeness in which everything participates, the ground that every separate thing is a face of.

And the divine is reciprocity: the living give-and-take that binds all things, living and not, into one belonging. To touch the divine is to feel that nothing stands alone, that we are made of everything and everything is made, in part, of us.

We do not have to believe in a god to meet the divine in this sense. We only have to notice, clearly, that we inter-are with all that is.

Two streams of the tradition carry this understanding. The first looks inward and finds that the many are one. The second looks outward and finds that the one lives as mutual relationship. They meet in the middle, and the practice is how we learn to feel them.

Tara Brach · Radical Acceptance

The word is a door, not a gate

In Radical Acceptance, Tara Brach describes the Buddhist practice of taking refuge, and in doing so quietly solves the problem this page keeps circling. The formal practice recites three lines: I take refuge in the Buddha, in the dharma, in the sangha. Brach names the three as “the Buddha (our awakened nature), the dharma (the path or the way) and the sangha (the community of spiritual aspirants).” What she does next is the useful part.

She does not treat the first refuge as a loyalty test. “Taking refuge in the Buddha,” she writes, “may be approached on various levels, and we can choose the way most meaningful to our particular temperament.” We might take refuge in the historical man who sat under the tree and met his fear. We might, if we are devotional by nature, take refuge in a presence: “Much like praying to Christ or the Divine Mother, we can take refuge in a being or presence that cares about our suffering.” Brach names her own substitution directly:

“In taking this first refuge, I sometimes say, ‘I take refuge in the Beloved’ and surrender into what I experience as the boundlessness of compassion. When I am feeling fear, I surrender it to the Beloved. By this, I am not trying to get rid of fear, but rather letting go into a refuge that is vast enough to hold my fear with love.” TARA BRACH, RADICAL ACCEPTANCE (BANTAM), “OPENING OUR HEART IN THE FACE OF FEAR,” P. 175

And then she turns it inward, so that even the presence is optional. “In the most fundamental way,” she writes, “taking refuge in the Buddha means taking refuge in our own potential for liberation.” After the Beloved, her next line is “I take refuge in this awakening heart mind,” letting go of “any notion that Buddha nature is something beyond or outside my awareness.” The refuge ends up in the least mystical place imaginable: “We are taking refuge in the truth of who we are.”

Why this matters for the divine

Brach is not saying the words are interchangeable because nothing means anything. She is saying the refuge is a real place, and that people come to it through the door their temperament opens. Buddha, the Beloved, Christ, the Divine Mother, awakening awareness: the noun changes, and the act of letting go into something vast enough to hold our fear does not. Read Ishvara pranidhana that way and surrender stops requiring a metaphysics we do not have. It asks instead what we are willing to stop carrying alone.

The second refuge helps too, because it needs no belief at all. Brach notes that dharma “means the truth, the way of things, the law of nature,” and that taking refuge in it is taking refuge in “the truth that everything within and around us is subject to change.” Impermanence is not a doctrine we accept. It is a fact we stop fighting. That is available to the theist and the atheist on identical terms.

The second doorway · energies and archetypes

The gods as essences to learn from

This is the door I use, so I will describe it carefully rather than sell it. Read this way, the deities of yoga are not beings who made the world. They are compressed accounts of forces that run through a life, given a face so they can be studied, remembered, and practiced.

The advantage is that the stories stay demanding. If Kali is a goddess I do not believe in, I can dismiss her. If Kali is the name for the ferocity that destroys what I have outgrown, including the parts of my self-image I am fond of, then she has something to ask of me on a Tuesday. Under the banyan tree we can see this at work: Shiva’s grief becoming Virabhadra is not a fact about the past, it is an account of what happens when sorrow finds its footing, and the Warrior poses put us in the shape of it. Shiva holding the poison at his throat is a teaching about what a practiced nervous system can do with something toxic: neither swallow it nor spit it at whoever is nearest.

The most developed version of this reading belongs to Ashok Bedi, a psychiatrist and Jungian analyst who treats the Hindu deities as a “latent code” of archetypes to be deciphered and used. In his hands the goddess is one force wearing several faces: “Shakti may manifest as lover (Laxmi), mother (Parvati), daughter (Saraswati), creator (Aditi) or destroyer (Kali).” His reading of Shiva’s blue throat, where Parvati holds her husband’s neck so he can neither swallow the poison nor spit it at the world, is the single clearest example I know of a myth doing real psychological work. Those readings now sit alongside the stories under the banyan tree, marked as the archetypal reading.

The honest risk of this door is that it can become a way of keeping the tradition at arm’s length, taking the psychology and leaving the devotion, which is a thing Western practitioners do to living traditions rather routinely. I try to hold that risk in view. Reading the gods as archetypes is a reading, and the people whose ancestors have prayed to them for millennia are not making a category error I have kindly corrected.

The first doorway · a presence that hears

If the divine is someone, not something

The texts this site draws on mostly assume this door. The Bhagavatam is not a psychology manual with a deity motif; it is a devotional work by people in a relationship with someone they loved. Reading it as anything else is a choice, and it should be named as one.

If this is your door, the practice is bhakti, and it is arguably the most direct one the tradition offers. Prahlada under poison and fire, Draupadi raising both hands when nothing else worked, the gopis dropping everything at the sound of the flute: none of these are metaphors to the people who told them. They are accounts of being answered. Ishvara pranidhana in this reading is exactly what it sounds like, surrender of your actions and their fruits to someone real, and the tradition holds it as a complete path on its own, needing no philosophy underneath it.

Nothing on this site is written to talk you out of that. Where I read energy and essence, you may read presence, and the pages will still work: the poses, the breath, the stories, and the ethics are the same. Brach’s point stands from your side of it too. The refuge is real; we differ on the name.

The third doorway · non-duality

The many are one: Advaita

The Upanishads, the contemplative heart of the Vedas, return again and again to a single discovery: behind the countless forms of the world there is one reality, Brahman, and that reality is also the innermost Self, Atman, in every being. The divine is the wholeness, and we are not outside it.

Īśāvāsyam idaṃ sarvam
All this, whatever moves in this moving world, is pervaded by the divine. The opening words of the Isha Upanishad: there is no place the sacred is absent.
Isha Upanishad, verse 1
Sarvaṃ khalvidaṃ brahma
All this is indeed Brahman. The world and its source are one reality seen in two ways.
Chandogya Upanishad 3.14.1
Tat tvam asi
Thou art That. The wholeness we seek is the very awareness that is seeking. The drop is told, gently, that it has always been the ocean.
Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.7, one of the four mahavakyas

This is Advaita, not-two. The divine is the seamless being, consciousness, and bliss that the meditation tradition calls sat-chit-ananda. Realizing it is the aim of contemplative yoga: to know directly that the witness in us and the ground of all things are one.

The second stream · reciprocity

The one lives as relationship: interbeing

If the first stream looks inward to find the one, the second looks outward and finds that the one is made entirely of relationship. Nothing exists by itself. Everything is woven of everything else, and that endless mutual belonging is itself the face of the divine.

Buddhist

Interbeing

Thich Nhat Hanh’s word for dependent co-arising: to be is to inter-be. In a sheet of paper he saw the cloud, the rain, the tree, the sun. Nothing can be, by itself alone.

Indra’s Net

A net stretching infinitely in all directions, a jewel at every knot, and each jewel reflecting every other. Touch one, and the whole net trembles. Each thing contains all things.

Living earth

Reciprocity

The give-and-take that sustains all life: breath traded with trees, water with clouds, nourishment with soil. To receive and to give back, in balance, is to live the divine as practice.

In this view the divine lives in the relationship itself, the breathing exchange between us and the tree, between the rain and the river, between one being and the next. Reverence becomes reciprocity: to honor the divine is to give back to the web that holds us.

The divine in practice

How this lives on the mat

Whichever door you came through, the practice from here is the same, which is the quiet argument of this whole page.

This is the second limb, Niyama, and its final observance is Ishvara pranidhana, surrender to the divine. Read in this way, surrender is the practice of remembering oneness and acting from reciprocity. It threads through the whole of yoga.

In the Yamas, the ethical roots, reciprocity becomes action: ahimsa, non-harming, is the simplest acknowledgment that another’s life is woven with our own. In Asana, the body learns that it is made of breath traded with the world. In meditation, the practitioner rests in the wholeness directly, until the boundary between self and all-that-is grows quiet.

Surrender to the divine, then, asks very little belief and a great deal of attention. It asks that we notice, in the pose and in the breath and in the meeting with another being, that we were never separate. The remembering is the worship.

Where the streams meet

Non-duality says the many are one. Reciprocity says the one lives as the loving relationship of the many. Held together, they describe a single truth from two directions: we are the whole, and we belong to it. The divine is both the ocean and the trading of the waves.

1.Where in an ordinary day do we most clearly feel that we inter-are with the world, that we are made of things outside ourselves?
2.If the divine is reciprocity, what are we receiving without yet giving back, and what might balance look like?
3.Tat tvam asi: thou art That. Sitting quietly, can we sense the awareness in us as the same awareness that lights all things?
