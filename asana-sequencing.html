Sequencing Asana

vinyasa krama · the intelligent ordering of practice

How a class is built: the arc that warms, builds, peaks, and returns the body to rest

Begin here

Feel the arc in ten minutes

Every principle on this page can be felt in a practice short enough to do right now. This miniature class holds the full arc of a longer one.

Practice · a class in miniature
Warm, build, unwind, rest
  1. Begin on hands and knees. Move through four slow rounds of Cat and Cow, letting the breath set the pace. This is the warming.
  2. Tuck the toes and lift the hips to Downward-Facing Dog. Pedal the feet, then walk them forward and roll up to standing.
  3. Step wide and open into Warrior II on the right side for five breaths, then the left. This is the building: the body working while fully warm.
  4. From the wide stance, hinge into a wide-legged forward fold for five breaths. The fold is the counterpose: the effort of the standing work released.
  5. Come down to the back, hug the knees, and take a supine twist to each side. The spine unwinds in both directions.
  6. Rest in Savasana for at least two minutes. The rest is part of the sequence; it is where the practice is absorbed.

Notice what the order did. The spine moved before it was loaded. The strongest work came in the middle, when the body was most prepared. Everything that was gathered was then released, and the practice ended below the level of effort where it began. That shape is the whole craft in one paragraph.

The shape of a class

The arc: warm, build, peak, descend, rest

A well-built class has the shape of a wave. Mark Stephens, whose Yoga Sequencing is the standard modern text, contrasts three ways classes get built: pure improvisation, fixed set sequences like Ashtanga's, and template teaching. Each has costs, and his answer is the one this page teaches: intentional sequencing, where anatomy, energetics, and the students in the room decide the order.

The older name for this intelligence is vinyasa krama. In T.K.V. Desikachar’s The Heart of Yoga, krama is the ordered step: every practice is built toward a chosen goal, each pose prepares the one after it, and the practice ends in a state the practitioner can carry out of the room. Sequencing in this older sense includes the descent. A class that climbs to its hardest pose and simply stops has told half a story; the poses that unwind the peak are as much a part of the krama as the ones that built it.

Amber Burke’s survey of alternative structures is a useful reminder that the peak-pose wave is one shape among several: poses can be repeated with different entrances, a path can be retraced in reverse, floor and standing work can alternate. The arc is the grammar to learn first, and like any grammar it can be played with once it is in the body.

How I sequence

Five rules I hold every sequence to

These rules came out of building and correcting real class plans, including the two sequences further down this page. Each one exists because I broke it once and felt the difference.

1. Every transition is written, never assumed

A sequence is a list of journeys, and the journeys deserve rows of their own. When two poses sit in different places, one standing and one supine, the steps between them belong in the plan. A transition left unwritten is a decision left to the most tired moment of the class.

2. The body must be able to get there

Before a sequence is final I trace the body through every consecutive pair of poses and ask whether the movement is physically possible. Lizard Pose into Happy Baby fails that test; there is no path to the back of the body without intermediate steps. If the trace stumbles, the sequence gains a pose rather than the student gaining an injury.

3. Vinyasa is the connective tissue

Between pose families, and especially between standing and floor work, a vinyasa (Plank, Low Plank, Upward-Facing Dog, Downward-Facing Dog) carries the body across the gap, resets the breath, and keeps the heat even. It is written into the sequence as its own labeled step.

4. Every deep action meets its counter

A deep backbend is followed by gentle flexion, a long twist by symmetry, an arm balance by release of the wrists and shoulders. The counterposes for every pose in the library are mapped in the family tree’s anatomy data, and the descent of a class is built from them.

5. A mandala physically rotates the mat

A mandala sequence is a journey in space, and the body travels it: facing the front of the mat, turning to one side, facing the back, and returning through the other side, a full circle. Both halves must be completed for the circle to close. The rotation is the meaning; the practice moves the way life does, in cycles that return.

The summit and the way down

Building toward a peak

A peak-pose class reads its summit backward. Choose the peak, name what it demands, and the first two thirds of the class assemble those demands one at a time.

Firefly Pose asks for open hamstrings, deep hip flexion, and shoulder-pressing strength, so a class built toward it opens the hamstrings in waves, floor first and standing second, and rehearses the shoulder work in stages long before the full pose appears. A forearm balance asks for shoulder stability and a fearless relationship with being upside down, so dolphin and forearm plank appear in the opening minutes, quietly rehearsing the peak while the body still thinks it is warming up. By the time the summit arrives, nothing about it is new; the class has already practiced every piece of it separately.

The way down mirrors the way up. Whatever the peak gathered, the descent releases: folds after deep hip work, gentle flexion after backbends, wrist release after arm balances, symmetry after asymmetry. The class ends in Savasana below the level of effort where it began, and the nervous system files the whole journey as one completed shape.
