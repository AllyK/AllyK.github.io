One Root, Many Branches

the styles of yoga, and where they sit on the path

Vinyasa, hatha, yin, restorative, and the rest: a genealogy that traces each style back to the texts it grew from and shows where it touches the eight limbs

Begin here

Why this page exists

Vinyasa gets most of the attention on this site, and for a plain reason: it is the practice I teach and the one I sequence. A student who takes a Yin class, a Restorative class, and an Ashtanga class in the same week is meeting three tempers of one tradition, and it helps to know how they are related. This page is a genealogy. It traces the styles you meet in a studio back through their teachers to the texts they all draw on, and it marks where each one touches the eight limbs.

A first thing worth saying plainly: these styles are not an extra limb. Almost all of them are ways of practicing asana, the third limb, usually leaning on pranayama, the fourth. In the older sense of the word, nearly all of them are hatha yoga, the yoga of the body set down in the fifteenth-century Hatha Yoga Pradipika. What separates them is temper: how fast they move, how long they hold, how much heat they build, and which tissue or state of mind they work. I have marked each style on a warm-to-cool spectrum, from the most heating and active to the most cooling and receptive.

Warming, active Cooling, receptive

A second thing, in keeping with how this site handles claims. The styles below are mostly twentieth-century creations, however ancient they feel. Most descend from one man, Tirumalai Krishnamacharya, teaching in a Mysore palace in the 1930s, and the scholarship of Mark Singleton and Elizabeth De Michelis shows how much modern posture practice owes to that recent moment and to the physical culture around it. I name founders and dates so the recent things are not mistaken for the eternal ones, and where a style rests on a mechanism the evidence does not support, I say so.

Read the tree from the top. Three root texts feed a trunk, the trunk divides into lineages, and each lineage carries the styles that grew from it. Open any style to see its source, its principles, the limbs it reaches toward, and a short example of how a class in that style is shaped.

The taproots

Three texts the whole tree draws on

No single scripture founded modern yoga. Three older texts sit underneath all of it: one gives the paths, one gives the eight limbs, and one gives the body.

Bhagavad Gita
the song of the paths
A dialogue between Arjuna and Krishna inside the Mahabharata epic. It frames yoga as paths that can be walked: karma yoga, the yoga of selfless action; bhakti yoga, of devotion; and jnana yoga, of knowledge. Dating is contested, roughly the second century BCE to the second century CE.
Bhagavad Gita · Britannica
Yoga Sutras
of Patanjali
The source of the eight-limbed path, ashtanga, and of yoga defined as citta-vritti-nirodha, the stilling of the mind's turnings. It is a philosophy and meditation text; it prescribes no fitness postures. Authorship and date are unsettled, with estimates spanning the second century BCE to the fifth century CE.
Patanjali · Britannica
Hatha Yoga Pradipika
Svatmarama, c. 15th century
The synthesizing classic of premodern hatha, the yoga of the body. Its four chapters cover asana, pranayama, mudra and bandha, and samadhi, along with diet and kundalini. Nearly every postural style on this page is, in the old sense, its descendant.
Hatha Yoga Pradipika · Yogic Studies
Tirumalai Krishnamacharya
1888–1989 · Mysore

The trunk of most modern posture practice. A Sanskrit and philosophy scholar, Krishnamacharya built a dynamic system of postures linked by repeating sun salutations while teaching at the Jaganmohan Palace in Mysore from 1933. His guiding tenet was that practice must be fitted to the time, the place, and the individual student. Three of his students carried three different readings of that teaching out into the world, and much of the tree below grows from him.

This is also where honesty matters most. Krishnamacharya's Mysore is recent history, and the historians Mark Singleton and Elizabeth De Michelis have shown how much of what we now call ancient yoga was shaped in that century, in conversation with gymnastics and physical culture. The practices are real and worth doing; their claimed antiquity is often younger than it sounds.

Krishnamacharya · Wellcome Collection Singleton, Yoga Body · Oxford University Press De Michelis, A History of Modern Yoga

Iyengar and its offspring
Iyengar YogaB.K.S. Iyengar, 1918–2014 · PuneOpen +Close −
Root

B.K.S. Iyengar was Krishnamacharya's brother-in-law and student. He spent some seventy-five years refining a method of alignment at the institute in Pune.

Principles

Precision, timing, and sequencing, with props such as belts, blocks, ropes, and blankets so that any body can find the shape of a pose. Postures are held long, alignment is studied closely, and the whole system is grounded in Patanjali's eight limbs.

Reaches toward
AsanaPranayamaDharana
A class in this style

Standing poses held for many breaths, each corrected in detail → props brought in to deepen or support → inversions such as headstand and shoulderstand → seated work and a long, precise Savasana.

Sources
Iyengar Yoga National Association
Restorative YogaJudith Hanson Lasater, from Iyengar's propsOpen +Close −
Root

Grows out of Iyengar's therapeutic use of props. Judith Hanson Lasater, who studied with Iyengar for about twenty-five years, shaped it into a distinct practice and set it down in her book Relax and Renew in 1995.

Principles

Fully supported, passive poses held for long minutes on bolsters and blankets, so the body can release completely. The aim is deep rest and a downshift of the nervous system. The pose is brought to the person rather than the person to the pose.

Reaches toward
PratyaharaDhyana
A class in this style

Supported reclined bound angle → supported forward fold over a bolster → legs up the wall → supported gentle backbend → a very long Savasana. Four or five poses can fill an hour.

Sources
Lasater on Iyengar · Yoga JournalRelax and Renew · Penguin Random House
Anusara YogaJohn Friend, 1997 · from IyengarOpen +Close −
Root

A senior Iyengar teacher, John Friend, added a heart-centered, non-dual Tantric philosophy to Iyengar-style alignment and named the result Anusara, roughly "flowing with grace," in 1997.

Principles

Five Universal Principles of Alignment layered over a devotional, life-affirming Tantric outlook. The practice pairs careful alignment with an intention of opening to the divine.

Reaches toward
AsanaThe Divine
Sources
Anusara School of Hatha Yoga
A note on its history

Anusara's founder resigned in 2012 after allegations of sexual relationships with students and employees and the freezing of the staff pension fund, and most of its senior teachers left. The teachers who stayed rebuilt the school without him. The alignment principles are useful; I keep the founder's conduct in view rather than out of it, and I believe those who came forward. Ashtanga and the flow it fathered
Ashtanga Vinyasa YogaK. Pattabhi Jois, 1915–2009 · MysoreOpen +Close −
Root

Krishnamacharya's student K. Pattabhi Jois systematized a vigorous, fixed practice in Mysore, tracing its sequences to a text called the Yoga Korunta whose provenance is now debated.

Principles

Fixed progressive series, Primary through Advanced. Vinyasa means breath-synchronized movement; ujjayi breath and the bandhas build internal heat, and drishti gaze points steady the mind. Physically demanding and the same each day.

Reaches toward
AsanaPranayamaDharana
A class in this style

Sun Salutation A and B, several rounds each → the standing sequence → the seated Primary Series with a vinyasa between poses → backbends → the finishing sequence of inversions → rest.

Sources
K. Pattabhi Jois Ashtanga Yoga Shala
Vinyasa Flow & Power YogaBeryl Bender Birch & Bryan Kest, from the 1990sOpen +Close −
Root

An unfixed, freely sequenced descendant of Ashtanga. Beryl Bender Birch, who learned Ashtanga through a direct Jois student, coined "power yoga" in late-1980s New York and published Power Yoga in 1995. Bryan Kest built a parallel Power Yoga on the West Coast and opened one of the first donation-based studios.

Principles

Breath-linked movement like Ashtanga, but the teacher composes a fresh sequence toward a peak rather than following a set series. Athletic, warming, and adaptable to the room. This is the style whose craft the rest of this site's Asana section is built around.

Reaches toward
SequencingAsanaPranayama
A class in this style

See the two worked class plans on the Sequencing page, each shown phase by phase: the wave that warms, builds, peaks, and returns the body to rest.

Sources
Birch, Power Yoga · Simon & SchusterThe birth of power yoga · KripaluBryan Kest · Power Yoga
Desikachar and the individualized turn
ViniyogaT.K.V. Desikachar · Chennai, 1976Open +Close −
Root

Krishnamacharya's son T.K.V. Desikachar carried on his father's later, most individualized teaching and founded the Krishnamacharya Yoga Mandiram in Chennai in 1976. The label "Viniyoga" is often associated in the United States with his student Gary Kraftsow.

Principles

Practice shaped to the single student's body, breath, age, and aim. Breath leads the movement, and there is no fixed sequence. Strongly therapeutic in orientation.

Reaches toward
PranayamaAsana
Sources
Krishnamacharya Yoga Mandiram
Yoga Therapyclinical application · IAYT, 1989Open +Close −
Root

Not a single style but the clinical application of yoga to individual health conditions, drawing especially on the individualized Krishnamacharya and Viniyoga stream. It is anchored by the International Association of Yoga Therapists, IAYT, founded in 1989.

Principles

Asana, breath, meditation, and lifestyle fitted to a specific person and condition, with therapeutic rather than fitness goals. Practitioners credential through the C-IAYT certification and accredited training programs, and the field publishes its own peer-reviewed research.

Reaches toward
PranayamaMeditationPratyahara

The Sivananda line
From Swami Sivananda of Rishikesh and the Divine Life Society, a devotional, whole-limbed reading of yoga carried west.
Sivananda YogaSwami Vishnudevananda · from 1959Open +Close −
Root

Swami Sivananda of Rishikesh taught his disciple Swami Vishnudevananda, who systematized the practice and carried it to the West, founding the international Sivananda Yoga Vedanta Centres from 1959.

Principles

The Five Points of Yoga: proper exercise, proper breathing, proper relaxation, proper vegetarian diet, and positive thinking with meditation. A set sequence of twelve basic postures, with relaxation between them.

Reaches toward
PranayamaMeditationThe Divine
A class in this style

Sun Salutations and breathing → headstand → shoulderstand and plough → fish → seated forward fold → cobra, locust, bow → seated twist → standing poses → deep relaxation.

Sources
The Five Points of Yoga · Sivananda
Integral YogaSwami Satchidananda · New York, 1966Open +Close −
Root

Swami Satchidananda, ordained by Swami Sivananda, founded the Integral Yoga Institute in Manhattan in 1966 and famously opened the 1969 Woodstock festival.

Principles

A gentle hatha practice inside a larger synthesis of six branches, hatha, raja, bhakti, karma, jnana, and japa, with an emphasis on selfless service and interfaith understanding. Its motto: "an easeful body, a peaceful mind, and a useful life."

Reaches toward
YamaThe DivineMeditation

Other guru lineages
Styles that arrive through their own teachers rather than through Mysore or Rishikesh.
Kripalu YogaSwami Kripalu → Amrit Desai · from 1966Open +Close −
Root

Named for Swami Kripalu, and brought to the United States by his disciple Amrit Desai, who founded the Yoga Society of Pennsylvania in 1966. It grew into the Kripalu Center for Yoga and Health in Stockbridge, Massachusetts. This is a separate guru lineage, not a Sivananda offshoot.

Principles

Meditation in motion. Attention turns inward, poses follow the body's natural flow of prana rather than being forced, and the practice moves in stages toward stillness. Nondogmatic and grounded in the eight limbs.

Reaches toward
PratyaharaDharanaMeditation
Sources
The life of Swami Kripalu
A note on its history

In 1994, revelations of sexual and financial improprieties by Amrit Desai led the community to call for his resignation, in the center's own telling. Kripalu remade itself afterward as a secular, board-governed retreat and education center. The practice outlived the guru model that started it, and I trust the community's account.

Our History · Kripalu
Kundalini YogaYogi Bhajan · 3HO, 1969Open +Close −
Root

As taught in the West, this is the codification Yogi Bhajan began teaching in the United States in 1968 and 1969, when he founded the organization known as 3HO. He presented it as rooted in older tantric and Sikh traditions, though it does not map cleanly onto a single documented lineage.

Principles

Set sequences called kriyas, strong pranayama such as breath of fire, mantra and chanting, mudra and bandha, all aimed at raising energy through the body. It spends far more of its time in breath, sound, and meditation than most postural styles.

Reaches toward
PranayamaDharanaThe Divine
A class in this style

Tuning in with mantra → warm-up and pranayama → a kriya, a fixed set of movement and breath toward one effect → deep relaxation → seated meditation with mantra → closing.

A note on its history

After Yogi Bhajan's death, an independent investigation commissioned by the organization itself, known as the Olive Branch report in 2020, heard hundreds of accounts and concluded he more likely than not sexually abused followers, including minors, alongside financial exploitation. Many practitioners have since kept the practices while parting from the man and his institutions. I name this plainly and believe those who came forward.

Olive Branch report · Religion News Service
Bikram & Hot YogaBikram Choudhury · 1973Open +Close −
Root

Bikram Choudhury credited his teacher Bishnu Ghosh of Calcutta, brother of Paramahansa Yogananda, and built a branded, heated system from 1973 in Los Angeles. "Hot yoga" is the broader, unbranded practice in a heated room that followed.

Principles

A fixed sequence of twenty-six postures and two breathing exercises, about ninety minutes, in a room heated to roughly 105 degrees Fahrenheit.

Reaches toward
AsanaPranayama
The claim that the heat "detoxifies" the body is marketing rather than established physiology. The heat does raise heart rate and perceived effort, and it raises the risk of overheating and dehydration, which is worth respecting.
A note on its history

Bikram Choudhury faced multiple lawsuits from women alleging sexual assault and harassment, a large civil judgment, and an arrest warrant, after which he left the United States. Hot yoga is widely taught now without his name or his scripted dialogue. I keep this history visible and believe those who came forward.

Bikram Choudhury · TIME
Grafts from other roots
Practices that draw on traditions outside the hatha line, a Taoist graft and a tantric one.
Yin YogaZink → Grilley → Powers · from the late 1980sOpen +Close −
Root

A graft of Taoist yoga and Chinese-medicine meridian theory onto a Western anatomy frame. Paul Grilley synthesized Daoist yoga learned from Paulie Zink with the energetics of Hiroshi Motoyama; Sarah Powers, an early student, added a meditative Buddhist emphasis and helped spread the name Yin.

Principles

Long passive floor holds, commonly three to five minutes, meant to reach the deep connective tissue and joints rather than the muscles. Stillness and meridian theory frame the practice.

Reaches toward
PratyaharaDhyana
A class in this style

Butterfly, held long → Dragon lunge, each side → Caterpillar forward fold → Sphinx or Seal → a reclined twist, each side → Savasana. Five or six shapes fill the hour.

The founder question is genuinely contested, and Paulie Zink has said modern Yin is not the whole of what he taught. The idea that long holds selectively remodel fascia or move energy along meridians is traditional framing, not settled physiology. The practice is still a fine way to work stiffness and stillness.
Sources
Paul Grilley · yinyoga.com
Yoga NidraSwami Satyananda, 1964 · iRest, Richard MillerOpen +Close −
Root

A tantric "yogic sleep," systematized by Swami Satyananda Saraswati of the Bihar School of Yoga, who said he revised an older tantric teaching and brought it into practice around 1964. Richard Miller later adapted it into iRest, used clinically for trauma and with veterans.

Principles

Done lying down and awake: a settling, a sankalpa or intention, a systematic rotation of awareness through the body, breath awareness, and guided imagery, resting in a witnessing stillness. It is a practice of the senses turning inward more than a practice of postures.

Reaches toward
PratyaharaDharana
A class in this style

Settle and relax the body → set a sankalpa → rotate awareness part by part → follow the breath → pairs of opposite sensations → imagery → return to the sankalpa and back.

Sources
Yoga Nidra Network · academic resourcesiRest · Richard Miller
Modern syntheses and the widening door
Recent forms that either braid several lineages together or work to open the practice to every body.
Jivamukti YogaSharon Gannon & David Life · New York, 1984Open +Close −
Root

An eclectic New York synthesis founded by Sharon Gannon and David Life in 1984, vinyasa-based and influenced by Ashtanga, with a strong scriptural and devotional overlay.

Principles

Five tenets carry the practice: ahimsa, nonviolence including vegetarianism and activism; bhakti, devotion; nada, sacred sound; dhyana, meditation; and shastra, scriptural study. A physically strong flow set inside an ethical frame.

Chair & Accessible YogaLakshmi Voelker, 1982 · Jivana Heyman, 2007Open +Close −
Root

Chair yoga was developed by Lakshmi Voelker, a Kripalu-trained teacher, from 1982, adapting hatha to a seated and supported form. The wider Accessible Yoga movement was founded by Jivana Heyman in 2007, arguing that the practice belongs to every body.

Principles

Postures, breath, and meditation adapted to a chair or to individual ability, so that age, disability, or illness is no barrier. As much a stance on equity in yoga as a set of shapes.

Reaches toward
AsanaPranayamaPratyahara

Prenatal Yogaa modified hatha practice · no single founderOpen +Close −
Root

Not a distinct lineage but hatha yoga modified for pregnancy, shaped by many teachers rather than one founder.

Principles

Gentle, supported postures with props, breathing, and relaxation fitted to each trimester. Deep compression, lying flat on the back for long, and heated rooms are set aside.

Reaches toward
AsanaPranayamaPratyahara
Guidance here is genuinely medical. A reputable clinical source is worth more than any studio's tradition, and hot yoga in particular is one to avoid in pregnancy.

Back to the path

Where the branches meet the limbs

Every style above carries a small row called Reaches toward. Those chips are the connection this section is about. A branch is a way of moving the body, and the limbs are the eight-fold path that body practice is meant to open onto. The chips name, for each style, which limbs it leans into most: some reach toward breath, some toward the inward turn of the senses, some toward the divine. Read them together and the tree stops being a list of studios and becomes a map of doorways into one house.

The cool end is a doorway

Look down the cooling end of the spectrum and something becomes clear. The quietest styles are where asana stops being exercise and becomes the next limb. In a long Yin hold or a fully supported Restorative pose, the senses draw inward on their own, and that inward turn is pratyahara. Yoga Nidra is almost pure pratyahara, practiced lying down. Kundalini spends most of its hour in breath and mantra, which are pranayama and the seed of dharana. The active styles have their own thresholds: the steady gaze, the drishti, of Ashtanga is a concentration practice, and the stillness at the end of any strong class is where dhyana can arrive.

The paths run alongside the limbs

The eight limbs are one map of yoga. The Bhagavad Gita draws an older one: not limbs but paths, the margas a life can walk. Karma yoga is the path of selfless action, jnana yoga the path of knowledge, raja yoga the meditative discipline that Patanjali sets out, and bhakti yoga the path of devotion. This page charts posture, so the paths sit underneath the tree rather than on it, but they surface wherever a style leans toward love and surrender rather than only toward the shape of the body. That is why Sivananda, Integral, Kundalini, Anusara, and Jivamukti each reach toward The Divine: they carry a devotional current, the path of bhakti, into a room full of postures. Bhakti, the yoga of devotion, is charted in full on The Divine.

So the styles are doors of different widths into the same house. Some come in through effort and heat, some through stillness and support, some through devotion, but the far rooms are shared: the drawing inward of the senses, the gathering of the mind, and the absorption the whole path is pointed at. Whichever branch you practice on, it is still the one tree, and its highest limbs are the same.
