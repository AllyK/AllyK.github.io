About

Dr. Ally Krupar · Rooted Action

Yoga in the West is often taught faster than it is understood. Poses arrive without their stories, breath techniques without their physiology, and philosophy without its sources. The Living Practice began as my own answer to that gap: a study notebook, built in public, where every claim about the practice is traced back to something that can be read, checked, and questioned.

Who I am

I am a researcher by training and by temperament. My work sits at the intersection of education, participatory action research, and communities affected by crisis, and it has appeared in venues including Forced Migration Review, the Journal on Education in Emergencies, and NORRAG. The habits of that work carry over to this site: name the source, show the evidence, report plainly what is uncertain, and treat the people a practice is meant to serve as its first authority.

I am also a yoga student in the middle of teacher training. I am completing all 500 hours within two years, because that is how I roll: I did my PhD in four. The pace is quick, and the standard is the same one I hold my research to. Everything on this site was built as part of that training, which means it is a record of learning in motion. Where I have gotten something wrong, I want to know, and the form at the bottom of every page is there for exactly that.

Rooted Action

Rooted Action is the umbrella for this work: the place where my research practice and my movement practice meet. The name says the method. Action that serves people grows from roots, from evidence, from tradition honestly engaged, and from the communities in which a practice lives. Under it sit The Living Practice, my work bringing movement and contemplative practice into education settings, and the research that keeps both honest.

This site

The Living Practice follows the eight limbs. The yamas are read with their sutras and the stories that teach them. The asana pages map pose families to the muscles that move them on the muscle diagram. The Cosmic Banyan matches stories from the Vedas with the poses and sequences that carry them. The breath, meditation, and dharana pages pair traditional instruction with the peer-reviewed literature. Scripture links go to primary sources such as Vedabase and the Valmiki Ramayana, and research links go to the journals themselves.

A note on authority: I write here as a student and a researcher. For the tradition itself, go to the sources linked throughout these pages and to teachers within living lineages. For medical questions, go to a clinician. This site will always show you where its claims come from so you can weigh them yourself.
