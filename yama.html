THE FIRST LIMB OF YOGA
Yama
the guiding principles · Yoga Sutras 2.30–2.31

The ground the practice stands on

Before posture, before breath, before meditation

Patanjali opens the eight limbs with five restraints. Sutra 2.30 names them: ahiṁsā-satya-asteya-brahmacarya-aparigrahā yamāḥ. Non-harming, truthfulness, non-stealing, wise use of energy, non-grasping. Sutra 2.31 raises the stakes: practiced without exception for birth, place, time, or circumstance, they become the mahāvratam, the great vow. Ethics comes first in Patanjali’s sequence. Posture, breath, and meditation are built on top of it.

The yamas are taught twice. Patanjali states them in aphorisms, one line each, with a result attached: sutras 2.35 through 2.39 describe what happens in the presence of a person in whom each restraint has become stable. The epics teach the same principles in blood, love, and impossible choices: Sita’s truth carried to its furthest edge, Bali giving away three worlds with his eyes open, Ravana seizing what can only be given. Those stories live under the Cosmic Banyan Tree, searchable by pose, archetype, and yama. This page is the ground. The stories are how it is walked.

A NOTE ON THE TEXT ITSELF

The Yoga Sutras have a biography of their own. Sanskrit historian David Gordon White traces how a text nearly out of circulation by the twelfth century became modern yoga’s central scripture in The Yoga Sutra of Patanjali: A Biography (Princeton, 2014). Stuart Sovatsky’s review in the Journal of Transpersonal Psychology is a short doorway into that scholarship. Holding both keeps the study honest: the principles are ancient, and our relationship to the text keeps being remade.

The five yamas

Each with its sutra, its Gita root, and its stories
Ahimsaahiṃsā
Do no harm
Ahiṁsā-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ tat-sannidhau vaira-tyāgaḥ. In the presence of one established in non-harming, hostility is abandoned.YOGA SUTRA 2.35

Reverence for life in thought, word, and deed, including toward your own body. The Gita lists ahimsa among the marks of knowledge (13.8–12), the divine qualities (16.2), and the austerity of the body (17.14). Then it complicates the principle. Arjuna refuses to fight and calls the refusal ahimsa. Krishna answers that the deepest harm can be refusing your own dharma (2.31–33). The first yama is a discipline of discernment as much as restraint.

IN THE STORIES

Shiva forgives Daksha after complete grief and complete ferocity (Srimad Bhagavatam, Canto 4): the restraint at ahimsa’s center. Krishna’s first answer holds the paradox: weapons cannot cut the soul (2.23), and grief at action differs from grief at harm.

ON THE MAT

Ahimsa begins with your own joints: honest range, a full warm-up, the kind adjustment over the impressive one. The disposition itself is trainable. In Weng and colleagues’ 2013 trial, two weeks of compassion practice increased altruistic behavior toward strangers and shifted the neural response to suffering.

Satyasatya
Live the truth
Satya-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ kriyā-phala-āśrayatvam. For one established in truthfulness, action and its fruit come to rest on what is said.YOGA SUTRA 2.36

Speaking what is, and beyond that, refusing to live inside a lie even when the lie is more comfortable. The Gita pairs satya with ahimsa among the divine qualities (16.2) and defines the austerity of speech: words that are truthful, pleasing, beneficial, unagitating (17.15). Truth and kindness travel together in this teaching. A truth flung as a weapon fails the austerity twice.

IN THE STORIES

Sita answers the second demand for proof by calling on the earth itself (Valmiki Ramayana, Uttara Kanda). Yudhishtir’s honesty is absolute, and a rigged dice game turns it against him. Prahlada, under poison and fire, keeps saying what he knows (Srimad Bhagavatam, Canto 7).

Asteyaasteya
Take only what is given
Asteya-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ sarva-ratna-upasthānam. For one established in non-stealing, all jewels approach.YOGA SUTRA 2.37

Asteya reaches past objects into time, credit, attention, and another’s freedom. The Gita says it plainly: one who enjoys what is given without offering anything back is stena eva saḥ, a thief (3.12). The whole third chapter frames life as a cycle of offering; hoarding is theft from that cycle. Research has measured the cycle’s other direction: in Dunn, Aknin and Norton’s 2008 experiments, money spent on others raised happiness more than money spent on oneself.

IN THE STORIES

Ravana seizes what can only be given, and Lanka burns for it. Bali gives everything, knowingly, and receives a kingdom lovelier than the three worlds (Srimad Bhagavatam, Canto 8). The dice game dresses theft in the costume of a fair contest.

Brahmacharyabrahmacarya
Spend energy on what matters
Brahmacarya-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ vīrya-lābhaḥ. For one established in brahmacharya, vigor is gained.YOGA SUTRA 2.38

Literally, walking with Brahman: the stewardship of vital force. The Gita’s meditator sits brahmacāri-vrate sthitaḥ, steady in the vow, mind fixed and fearless (6.14). The same discipline appears in the austerity of the body (17.14) and at the practitioner’s final journey (8.11). Some lineages read celibacy. The broader reading is allocation: energy spent where devotion actually lives.

IN THE STORIES

Shiva holds both poles at once, total ascetic and total lover (Srimad Bhagavatam, Canto 4). Hanuman is the tradition’s living image of the vow: every gift, strength, scholarship, flight, poetry, placed in service, nothing leaking sideways (Valmiki Ramayana, Sundara Kanda).

Aparigrahaaparigraha
Hold life with open hands
Aparigraha-sthairye janma-kathaṁtā-sambodhaḥ. When non-grasping is steady, the how and why of birth is understood.YOGA SUTRA 2.39

The Gita’s meditator lives aparigrahaḥ, free of possessiveness (6.10). The liberated actor works tyakta-sarva-parigrahaḥ, all sense of ownership released (4.21). This is the yama closest to the Gita’s core instruction: you have a right to the work alone, never to its fruits (2.47). Psychology has begun to measure the trait: Sahdra, Shaver and Brown’s 2010 nonattachment scale tracks it against adaptive functioning and wellbeing.

IN THE STORIES

Bali gives the three worlds away with full knowledge of the cost (Srimad Bhagavatam, Canto 8). Rama sets down a kingdom like a garment. Chapter 15 finishes the arc: cut the deeply rooted tree with the axe of detachment, asaṅga-śastreṇa dṛḍhena (15.3–4).

What the research sees

Peer-reviewed work on the ethical limbs and the text that carries them

The yamas are the least studied limb of yoga in modern research. A starting shelf:

From Yama to Samyama
INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF YOGA, 2016 · 9(2), 95–96
Frames yama as the start and samyama as the culmination of one continuous arc, and argues the eight limbs progress together rather than in sequence: as asana is practiced, the attitude of the yamas is practiced inside it.
Impact of yama and niyama on psychospiritual factors in young adults: a randomized controlled trial
XU, ITAGI & THAIYAR · INT. J. YOGA (PHILOSOPHY, PSYCHOLOGY & PARAPSYCHOLOGY), 2021 · 9(1), 32–39
One hundred young adults, randomized to three months of yama-niyama training or control. The training group gained on sattva and dropped on rajas and tamas (Vedic Personality Inventory, P<0.001). Early evidence that the ethical limbs are trainable, with measurable change in disposition.
Compassion training alters altruism and neural responses to suffering
WENG ET AL. · PSYCHOLOGICAL SCIENCE, 2013 · 24(7), 1171–1180
Two weeks of compassion practice increased altruistic behavior toward strangers and changed how the brain responded to images of suffering. The closest laboratory analogue to ahimsa as Patanjali frames it: a cultivated disposition, strengthened by repetition.
Three riffs upon The Yoga Sutra of Patanjali: A Biography
SOVATSKY · JOURNAL OF TRANSPERSONAL PSYCHOLOGY, 2015 · 47(1), 148
A review essay on David Gordon White’s history of the Yoga Sutras: how the text was composed, lost, and rebuilt into modern yoga’s scripture. Useful for holding the yamas with devotion and historical honesty at once.
