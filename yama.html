THE FIRST LIMB OF YOGA
Yama
the guiding principles · Yoga Sutras 2.30–2.31

The ground the practice stands on

Before posture, before breath, before meditation

Patanjali, the father of yoga, set out the five restraints as the fundamental principles of yoga: ahiṁsā (non-harming), satya (truthfulness), asteya (non-stealing), brahmacarya (wise use of energy), and aparigrahā (non-grasping). These principles must be practiced without exception for birth, place, time, or circumstance, becoming mahāvratam, the great vow. Posture, breath, and meditation are built on top of ethics.

Sutras 2.35 through 2.39 describe what happens in the presence of a person in whom each restraint has become stable. The epics teach the same principles in archetypes: Sita’s truth carried to its edge, Bali knowingly releasing three worlds, Ravana seizing what can only be given. Those stories live under the Banyan Tree, and are searchable by pose, archetype, and yama.

ORIGINAL TEXT NOTES

The philosophy of the Yoga Sutras is the foundation of modern yoga, and its history is a broken and rebuilt one. The text fell out of circulation for centuries before modern, and predominantly Western, practitioners extracted it, added to it, and raised it into yoga’s central scripture. David Gordon White traces that whole arc in The Yoga Sutra of Patanjali: A Biography (Princeton, 2014). The principles are ancient, but how they are kept is remade and reinterpreted in practice.

The five yamas

Each with its sutra, its Gita root, and its practice
Ahimsaahiṃsā
Do no harm
Tap to open the teaching
Ahiṁsā-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ tat-sannidhau vaira-tyāgaḥ. In the presence of one established in non-harming, hostility is abandoned.YOGA SUTRA 2.35

The Gita lists ahimsa among the marks of knowledge (13.8–12), the divine qualities (16.2), and the austerity of the body (17.14). But it is a complicated principle. Arjuna refuses to fight and calls the refusal ahimsa. Krishna answers that the deepest harm can be refusing our own dharma (2.31–33). The first yama is a discipline of discernment as much as restraint.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, ahimsa begins with our own journey: honoring our motion, our body’s signals, and doing no harm to ourselves with criticism, pressure, or comparison. In meditation, it is the gentleness that meets a wandering mind, each return to the breath made kindly. In daily life, it shapes how we speak to ourselves and others, how we handle conflict, and how we treat every living thing our day touches.

Satyasatya
Live the truth
Tap to open the teaching
Satya-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ kriyā-phala-āśrayatvam. For one established in truthfulness, action and its fruit come to rest on what is said.YOGA SUTRA 2.36

Satya is speaking what is, and refusing to live inside a lie even when the lie is more comfortable. The Gita defines satya as carefully choosing words that are truthful, pleasing, beneficial, and do the most good (17.15). Truth and kindness travel together in this teaching so truth used as a weapon is not practicing satya.

IN PRACTICE

Similar to ahimsa, practicing satya in movement is being true to ourselves and our path. Whether that means using props or returning to our breathing, the goal is not to get to the most difficult pose, but to embody our felt truth. In meditation, satya is honest witnessing: seeing what arises as it is, free of decoration or denial. In daily life, it is choosing words that are true, kind, and useful together, and living so the outside of our life matches the inside.

Wisdomlib: Satya
Asteyaasteya
Take only what is given
Tap to open the teaching
Asteya-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ sarva-ratna-upasthānam. For one established in non-stealing, all jewels approach.YOGA SUTRA 2.37

Asteya reaches past objects into time, credit, attention, and another’s freedom. The Gita says one who enjoys what is given without offering anything back is a thief (3.12). The whole third chapter frames life as a cycle of offering; hoarding is theft from that cycle. There’s research that backs up the concept: Dunn, Aknin and Norton (2008) found that when participants spent money on others they felt more happiness than when they spent money on themselves.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, practicing asteya reminds us not to over extend ourselves and take from our energy that could lead to injury, to steal another’s progress by comparing their journey to our own, and to share space so everyone has the same freedom to move. In meditation, asteya lets each breath be enough; grasping after a different experience steals the one that is here. In daily life, it means honoring people’s time, crediting ideas to their sources, listening without taking over, and taking from the earth only what can be returned.

Brahmacharyabrahmacarya
Spend energy on what matters
Tap to open the teaching
Brahmacarya-pratiṣṭhāyāṁ vīrya-lābhaḥ. For one established in brahmacharya, vigor is gained.YOGA SUTRA 2.38

The Gita’s meditator sits brahmacāri-vrate sthitaḥ, steady in the vow, mind fixed and fearless (6.14). The brahmacarya discipline also appears in the austerity of the body (17.14), sometimes read as celibacy, and at the practitioner’s final journey (8.11). Broadly, in brahmacarya, energy is self-regulated and focused.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, brahmacarya is the most direct principle guiding us to avoid excess in our practice and to focus on our self-regulation. In meditation, it is the steady vow of 6.14: energy gathered toward one point rather than scattered across every distraction. In daily life, it asks where our vitality actually goes, screens, worry, overcommitment, and returns it to what we most value.

Wisdomlib: Brahmacharya
Aparigrahaaparigraha
Hold life with open hands
Tap to open the teaching
Aparigraha-sthairye janma-kathaṁtā-sambodhaḥ. When non-grasping is steady, the how and why of birth is understood.YOGA SUTRA 2.39

The Gita’s meditator lives aparigrahaḥ, free of possessiveness (6.10). The liberated actor works tyakta-sarva-parigrahaḥ, all sense of ownership released (4.21). This is the yama closest to the Gita’s core instruction: we have a right to the work alone, never to its fruits (2.47). Psychology has begun to measure the trait: Sahdra, Shaver and Brown’s 2010 nonattachment scale tracks it against adaptive functioning and wellbeing.

IN PRACTICE

In movement, aparigraha releases yesterday’s pose: the body arrives different each day, and the practice meets it where it is. In meditation, it is the letting go inside every exhale, thoughts and states allowed to pass without being kept. In daily life, it is holding possessions, plans, and people with open hands, doing the work fully and releasing the outcome.

What the research sees

Peer-reviewed work on the ethical limbs and the text that carries them

The yamas are the least studied limb of yoga in modern research. A starting shelf:

From Yama to Samyama
INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF YOGA, 2016 · 9(2), 95–96
Argues that each of the eight limbs of yoga need to be practiced together, which is the basis of my approach inviting you to “begin anywhere” through the eight limbs.
Impact of yama and niyama on psychospiritual factors in young adults: a randomized controlled trial
XU, ITAGI & THAIYAR · INT. J. YOGA (PHILOSOPHY, PSYCHOLOGY & PARAPSYCHOLOGY), 2021 · 9(1), 32–39
One hundred young adults, randomized to three months of yama-niyama training or control. The training group gained on sattva and dropped on rajas and tamas (Vedic Personality Inventory, P<0.001). Early evidence that the ethical limbs are trainable, with measurable change in disposition.
Compassion training alters altruism and neural responses to suffering
WENG ET AL. · PSYCHOLOGICAL SCIENCE, 2013 · 24(7), 1171–1180
Two weeks of compassion practice increased altruistic behavior toward strangers and changed how the brain responded to images of suffering. The closest laboratory analogue to ahimsa as Patanjali frames it: a cultivated disposition, strengthened by repetition.
The Yoga Sutra of Patanjali: A Biography
WHITE · PRINCETON UNIVERSITY PRESS, 2014
The history of the text itself: composed, lost for centuries, and rebuilt into modern yoga’s central scripture, largely by Western revivalists who extracted from it and added to it. The reason this page holds the yamas with devotion and historical honesty at once.
